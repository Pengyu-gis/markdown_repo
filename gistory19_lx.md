**Xiao Li** is an Assistant Professor and PhD advisor at the Department of Land Surveying and Geo-Informatics (LSGI), The Hong Kong Polytechnic University, and an Honorary Research Fellow at the Transport Studies Unit (TSU), University of Oxford. He earned his PhD in Geography from Texas A&M University (TAMU). Before joining PolyU, he served as a Senior Research Fellow at Oxford's TSU and the "Bryan Warren" Junior Research Fellow at Linacre College from 2022 to 2025. He was also an Associate Researcher at the Texas A&M Transportation Institute (TTI) and a Visiting Scholar at Rice University's Kinder Institute for Urban Research.

His research interests lie at the intersection of Geographic Information Science (GIS), spatial data science, and transport geography. He leads the **GIFT Lab** (Geospatial Intelligence for Transport), a research group dedicated to developing geocomputational algorithms, applications, and tools that integrate emerging geospatial data sources, theories, and computational techniques to enhance the safety, accessibility, and sustainability of transport systems. From an applied science perspective, his research explores the value of emerging geospatial data sources—such as smartphone data, connected vehicle data, and street view imagery—in addressing complex transport challenges. From a social science perspective, he examines the interaction between people and transport systems, evaluating how new transport technologies and policies affect diverse social groups and communities. He currently serves as a member of the Standing Committee on Geospatial and Visual Analytics (AED14) of the Transportation Research Board (TRB), the Management Committee of the GIScience Research Group of the Royal Geographical Society (RGS-IBG), and the Board of Directors of the Chinese Professionals in Geographic Information Sciences (CPGIS).

In the landscape of Geographic Information Science (GIS), transport has always been a dynamic and complex variable. From Interstate 45 in Texas, USA, to the humanistic alleys of Oxford, UK, to the urban jungle of Victoria Harbour in Hong Kong, Dr. Xiao Li's research is rooted at the intersection of GIS, spatial data science, and transport geography. Leading his self-founded GIFT Lab (Geospatial Intelligence For Transport), he is attempting to provide new solutions for future urban mobility through spatial intelligence. In a late-2025 interview with the GIStory team, he emphasized that technology is never a cold algorithm, but rather a pathway toward social equity, traffic safety, and urban inclusivity.


## 01 A Research Trajectory Discovered from Road Bumps
Dr. Li's connection with transport geography was not a fateful love at first sight, but rather a "two-way journey" that gradually deepened through continuous exploration and adaptation.

The starting point of this academic journey stemmed from a serendipitous, life-inspired research moment. During his master's studies, while traveling with his advisor, Professor Ruizhi Chen (now at The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen), the violent vibration of their vehicle passing over a bumpy road sparked curiosity: could the sensors in smartphones, originally designed to balance the screen, be repurposed as detectors for monitoring road quality?

This seemingly minor intuitive spark not only gave rise to Dr. Li's master's thesis and his first academic publication, but also allowed him to intuitively feel the wondrous connection between geographic data and the physical world for the first time. Recalling the experience in the interview, he said: "If, at the moment I undertake a study, I can already envision the second, third, and even fourth extensions of it, then this is likely the right direction." For him, choosing transport was not an instantaneous decision, but a process of continuously verifying fit through practice.

This pragmatic research philosophy guided him from individual sensor studies during his master's to a broader research domain during his PhD—crowdsourcing and Volunteered Geographic Information (VGI). He realized that continuous monitoring of urban road networks could not rely on solo efforts, but required the wisdom of the crowd. However, while crowdsourced data offers significant advantages in volume, its application is often constrained by the limitations of low-cost sensors: missing precision, data redundancy, and uneven spatial distribution. In his representative PhD work, he returned to the essence of GIS, using geospatial mining techniques to transform chaotic crowdsourced signals into quantifiable road surface quality assessment indicators. This approach not only effectively identified overall pavement conditions but also precisely localized instantaneous road damage events. This intellectual evolution—from acquiring multi-source heterogeneous data to mining high-level knowledge—became the core logic of his subsequent research and further expanded the application of geographic data in diverse fields such as traffic safety assessment, facility management, congestion monitoring, risk perception, and human mobility analysis.

## 02 Academic Experiences and Reflections Across Three Continents

From the vast lands of Texas, USA, to the academically humanistic halls of Oxford, UK, to the hyper-efficient international metropolis of Hong Kong, Dr. Xiao Li's academic trajectory spanning three continents represents not merely geographic displacement, but the interweaving and fusion of three distinctly different academic paradigms.

In Dr. Li's observation, the American GIS community often stands at the forefront of technological innovation. During his time at Texas A&M University (TAMU), he deeply sensed a technology-driven atmosphere: from generative models to GeoAI, American scholars play a leading role in the cross-disciplinary fusion of computational science and geographic information science. Student research often focuses on cutting-edge areas such as supercomputing and GeoAI. Correspondingly, the American training system emphasizes accumulation: PhD students typically spend their first two years completing foundational and elective courses, passing qualifying exams to solidify their research capabilities. In his view, this model builds a stable foundational scaffold for subsequent research innovation.

In contrast, his experience at Oxford University introduced a stronger sense of humanistic care into his academic work. Although Oxford's transport geography research also employs GIS technology, it does not let technology dominate; rather, it emphasizes returning to the fundamental inquiries of geography and sociology. Take accessibility as an example: in traditional engineering perspectives, it is often simplified into a distance function; but from a sociological angle, we must further ask—how is accessibility defined? *Who* is moving? *Why* are they moving? The logic behind rigid demands such as commuting or medical access fundamentally differs from that of flexible demands like an afternoon stroll. Regarding student training, Dr. Li observed that Oxford's system assumes students are already well-trained, skipping the coursework phase to enter research directly, emphasizing the refinement of research logic through long-term theoretical discussion and writing.

Today, conducting research and teaching at The Hong Kong Polytechnic University, Dr. Li senses an academic atmosphere that blends characteristics of both the American and British systems. Hong Kong, as an extremely fast-paced city, fosters an equally efficient academic environment where students are generally diligent—it is not uncommon to see office lights still on at eleven at night. Based on these cross-cultural observations and reflections, Dr. Li has gradually developed a mentorship approach that balances pace with foundational rigor. He believes that, regardless of the academic system or cultural environment, solidifying basic skills and clarifying research concepts are always insurmountable checkpoints on the research path and indispensable cornerstones for academic development. At the same time, he hopes that every student in his team can find their own research rhythm during the PhD stage. Although peer pressure is omnipresent, he firmly believes that "every flower blooms in its own time." In the long run of research, there is no need to blindly chase others or doubt oneself—neither relaxing standards nor giving up possibilities, taking solid steps every day and living happily every day, one will eventually reach their own moment of bloom.

When discussing PhD qualifying exams in the interview, he mentioned the importance of regularly organizing research thoughts: "Perhaps it is precisely in constrained situations like exams that people are forced to rethink their research process. When you write down your ideas, make them into a PPT, and present them, the process itself helps you organize your thoughts." He expects students, within one year of enrollment, to form a clear understanding of their research topic through systematic literature review and solid foundational training, to define the feasible boundaries of their research direction, and to clarify which paths are worth exploring deeply and which aspects should be cautiously avoided.

## 03 Three Major Research Directions of GIFT Lab

<p align="center">
    <img src="https://pub-b7009020d42e47bf93fb9f6c95502a87.r2.dev/gisphere_%E8%AE%BF%E8%B0%88/lixiao/6.jpg">
</p>

&gt; **Figure 5** GIFT Lab Logo

Dr. Li envisions future mobility as a complex system driven by spatial intelligence, with its core goal being the realization of safety, efficiency, comfort, and inclusivity. To realize this vision, the GIFT Lab he leads is currently advancing the following three research directions:

### Traffic Safety: From Post-Hoc Governance to A Priori Warning

Traditional traffic safety research often exhibits a reactive lag—researchers typically summarize patterns based on existing accident data and then guide road improvements. However, this model struggles to keep pace with rapidly changing cities:

&gt; "If a new district just opened, do we really have to wait five more years to accumulate enough accident data before starting to improve the roads?"

Dr. Li's team shifts the research perspective from post-hoc statistics to pre-event identification, mapping potential traffic risks using heterogeneous data such as sensor data, trajectory information, driving behavior records, and social media sentiment. Through this system, they can identify high-risk road segments before accidents occur, enabling preventive optimization of road facilities.

### Road Assets: Fine-Grained Digitization of Traffic Facilities

Despite the grand visions of smart city planning, existing geographic data still shows significant gaps in the fine-grained coverage of micro-level traffic facilities. For example: What is the actual width of an intersection? What are the specific specifications of traffic signals? Are road signs aging and fading? Are some sign placements reasonable and compliant? He argues: "The current granularity of urban digitization remains too coarse to support refined planning and management." To address this, his team is leveraging street view imagery and Geospatial Artificial Intelligence (GeoAI) algorithms to automate the identification and analysis of micro-level road assets, filling data gaps and enhancing the precision of digital characterization of road space.

### The Essence of Mobility: Transport Inclusivity in Human-Environment Interaction

During the interview, Dr. Li asked: "Why could we ride bicycles to school when we were children, while most primary and secondary school students today need to be picked up by their parents?" He observes that modern urban road space is often excessively oriented toward motor vehicles, while neglecting the needs of marginalized groups. Whether it is people with low education, people with mobility disabilities, or the elderly and children, they may all face severe mobility barriers in vehicle-dominated urban spaces. He hopes that some of GIFT Lab's research can return to the essence of mobility, exploring the dynamic interaction between people and their environment. He and his students attempt to quantify the differentiated experiences of diverse groups within the same road system, hoping that through technical means, policymakers will realize that achieving true transport inclusivity cannot stop at providing standardized facilities; rather, it should strive to build transport systems and urban spaces that can respond to differentiated needs, allowing different residents to obtain safe and comfortable travel experiences.




## 04 Technology and Ethics: Moving Cautiously in the GeoAI Era

When discussing the currently highly anticipated GeoAI technology, Dr. Li described himself in the interview as still an "explorer and learner." In his view, the core value of GeoAI does not lie in the simple stacking of algorithms, but in its powerful information transformation capability—by fusing multi-source geospatial big data with unprecedented computing power, it can rapidly convert previously difficult-to-utilize massive, heterogeneous raw data (such as mobile phone trajectories, connected vehicle records, street view imagery, etc.) into analyzable geographic information, thereby achieving dual leaps in processing speed and analytical scale.

However, the rapid development of technology also brings ethical and privacy concerns. Dr. Li believes that in transport research involving social dimensions, information about "people" is often crucial, because understanding *who* is moving and *why* they are moving is key to revealing the social mechanisms behind many traffic phenomena. His previously proposed concept of "personalized traffic safety services" is based precisely on this philosophy—respecting the unique patterns and needs of individual drivers. At the same time, he believes researchers should strictly adhere to ethical review procedures such as IRB, use personal data cautiously, and ensure that the original intention of GeoAI is to serve society rather than infringe on privacy. When discussing the collection and acquisition of research data, he argues that academia and industry should achieve "demand matching." Commercial companies collect data based on cost-benefit considerations, while researchers need long-term, precise, and clearly defined data. He suggests that academic needs should be integrated at the early stages of data production, forming a sustainable cooperation cycle through supply-demand alignment, thereby providing a more solid and stable data foundation for urban governance and public policy research.



## 05 Writing Diligently, Advancing Steadily: Three Pieces of Advice for Young Scholars

As a young scholar who grew from being an international student, Dr. Li shared three personal reflections with the readers of GISphere—especially those at the early stages of their research careers:

### Reshaping Research Perspectives Through Collision

He encourages students to step out of the laboratory, actively participate in academic conferences, and engage in in-depth exchanges with scholars from different backgrounds. During his time at Oxford, he collaborated with a social scientist on traffic safety for children with special needs. The collaborator raised a point: if a road is so complex in its environment that children dare not go out, then even with "zero accidents," it is by no means safe. This made him realize that the dimensions for evaluating traffic events are diverse—young scholars should learn to step outside single-indicator perspectives and redefine problems from the intersection of multiple disciplines.

### "A General on a March Does Not Chase Rabbits": Grasping the Main Research Thread

On the research path, new technologies and methods emerge endlessly. Dr. Li borrowed a saying to remind his students and himself: "A general on a march does not chase rabbits." During the PhD, one must clearly define and adhere to the main research thread. Those seemingly attractive but unrelated topics are often "rabbits by the roadside." Otherwise, although one may produce many publications, they will fail to form a systematic academic structure. Exploring new technologies should be a booster, not a detour that leads one astray.

### Dynamic Optimization: Distinguishing Hot Topics from Valuable Research

He advises students to regularly (every six months or a year) review and revise their research plans to maintain clarity amid technological waves. In his view: "If a technology or topic gradually quiets down after two years, it may only be a short-term hotspot; if numerous scholars continue to explore it deeply after two years, then it truly possesses long-term research value." Chasing hotspots may bring short-term attention and citations, but only by systematically organizing research and continuously digging into core issues can one produce scientifically impactful results with sustainable influence and accumulate genuine academic contributions.

## 06 Conclusion

At the end of the interview, Dr. Li spoke about his connection with GISphere. As one of the early participants in the community, he witnessed the platform grow from a volunteer project into a globally influential academic community. He particularly identifies with GISphere's core philosophy of serving students and wishes that, with the participation of more students, it will become an even more influential and warmer academic community.

Just as he advocates for a balanced and measured research rhythm, Dr. Xiao Li hopes that every student exploring in the coordinate system of geographic space can develop "quick wit" through an intense pace, while also generating creative insights through composed reflection. On their chosen paths, they should face every challenge directly and enjoy every beautiful moment.
