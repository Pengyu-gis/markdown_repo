# GIStory Issue 12 | Mu Lan: Exploring Health Geography for Practical Worldly Benefit

## Guest Introduction

Mu Lan is a Professor in the Department of Geography and Director of the GIS Certificate Program at the University of Georgia (UGA). She served as President of the International Association of Chinese Professionals in Geographic Information Sciences (CPGIS) in 2023. She received her bachelor's degree from the College of Urban and Environmental Sciences at Peking University, her master's degree from the Department of Geography and Planning at the University of Toledo, and her PhD from the Department of Landscape Architecture and Environmental Planning at the University of California, Berkeley. Her research has been supported by funding from the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), the National Science Foundation (NSF), and other agencies. Her research interests focus on health geography and geospatial analytical methods. Professor Mu's research group is named **GIS.HEAL** (GIScience for Health and Environment Analytics Lab). [Website: https://gisheal.franklinresearch.uga.edu]

## 01 Serendipity: An "Academic Drift" from Peking University to Berkeley

When Professor Mu Lan was pursuing her undergraduate degree in Environmental Science at the College of Urban and Environmental Sciences, Peking University, she did not foresee that she would one day become a scholar specializing in Geographic Information Systems (GIS). During her undergraduate years, her research focus was on environmental geochemistry, and her thesis explored the soil reduction capacity of hexavalent chromium. This research journey took her through the entire process—from collecting soil samples, conducting environmental chemistry experiments, to data analysis and paper writing. This experience gave Professor Mu a profound realization of her strong interest in environmental issues. At the same time, she discovered that she preferred **data analysis**, enjoying the process of uncovering hidden patterns from complex data, rather than being confined solely to laboratory experimental work.

Professor Tao Shu, who had returned from studying abroad at the University of Kansas, offered a spatial statistics course and shared his professional knowledge generously. During her undergraduate studies, Mu enrolled in this course and thus became acquainted with various geostatistical methods. It was based on this knowledge, combined with her study of Professor Wu Lun's geographic information systems course, that Mu ultimately chose to pivot toward **Geographic Information Science (GIScience)** for deeper research.

During her time at the University of Toledo, Professor Mu encountered two scholars who had a profound impact on her academic career. The first was **Professor Muraco**, Chair of the Geography Department—an authoritative expert in geospatial analysis. Professor Muraco's *Healthcare Planning Seminar* course had a far-reaching influence on Mu. The course covered four modules: multiple regression, factor analysis, discriminant analysis, and cluster analysis, laying a solid foundation for Mu's future research in geospatial methods and health geography. The other person who significantly influenced her career was **Professor Eboch**. Mu shared an interesting story with us: having just arrived in the U.S. from China, she still maintained the habit of taking afternoon naps, which was not common in American schools. As a result, in Professor Eboch's 1:00 PM class, following her long-established biological clock, she would often doze off, which made the teaching assistant at the time very angry. Fortunately, Professor Eboch showed understanding. Mu eventually adapted to this new learning rhythm, not only persevered but also achieved excellent grades. Later, when the university's GIS Center needed a summer intern, Professor Eboch proactively contacted Mu, providing her with a valuable research opportunity. In this internship position, Mu learned how to use GIS technology to solve real-world problems.

After completing her master's studies, Professor Mu found that she was still filled with exploratory passion for the field of geographic information science, so she decided to continue her studies and pursue a PhD. Since the University of Toledo did not have a corresponding doctoral program, she applied to **UC Berkeley** to continue her academic journey. On the Berkeley campus, Mu's research focus was on **computational geometry and its applications in environmental planning**, a field full of challenges and innovation. At that time, the Berkeley campus gathered numerous outstanding scholars in the GIS academic circle, providing Mu with an excellent environment for academic exchange and learning. Her PhD advisor, **Professor Radke**, was a highly forward-thinking and open-minded scholar who encouraged students to engage in broad academic exchanges and exploration. At the same time, Mu also learned a great deal about remote sensing science from **Professor Gong Peng**, who was also a member of Mu Lan's doctoral dissertation committee.

After completing her PhD, Mu Lan was fortunate to receive guidance, mentorship, and support from **Professor Wang Fahui**, **Professor McLafferty**, and many senior colleagues and peers in her work, deepening her understanding of research in health geography and applying GIScience knowledge and methods to health geography. Mu later also participated in research projects related to the National Institutes of Health (NIH). These valuable experiences gradually shaped her academic research direction.

## 02 Doing Research for Practical Worldly Benefit

When we discussed her research with Professor Mu, one term kept emerging in our minds—**"经世致用" (practical worldly benefit)**. This term perfectly embodies the core philosophy of Geographic Information Science (GIScience) as a highly application-oriented discipline, namely its close connection with human well-being. Professor Mu's work vividly demonstrates this point.

Although Professor Mu's research field primarily focuses on geospatial analytical methods, it actually extends far beyond a narrow domain. Her research spans multiple different fields, from natural environment studies to public health, social life, and even economic development. She applies geographical thinking and analytical tools to solve various important practical problems, demonstrating the tremendous potential of GIScience as an interdisciplinary field. Professor Mu's work not only reflects the breadth and depth of geography in practical applications but also showcases the immense value of GIS technology in promoting social progress and improving human welfare. Her research is undoubtedly the best interpretation of the concept of "practical worldly benefit."

Professor Mu pays close attention to the environment and health fields, believing they are among the most important issues in human social development. To highlight the importance of geospatial analysis in the health field, Professor Mu introduced to us a recent study she conducted with her doctoral student **Yang Jue** (currently a postdoctoral researcher at Brown University). The inspiration for this research came from their observation of multiple regions in the United States: despite dense populations in these areas, facilities suitable for children and adolescents to participate in physical activities, such as gymnasiums, were relatively scarce and unevenly distributed. Based on this, Professor Mu and her students and colleagues applied the **five-dimensional indicator system** from healthcare planning to the evaluation of outdoor sports venues. These five dimensions are **Availability, Accessibility, Accommodation, Affordability, and Acceptability**. They used this system to analyze the availability of outdoor sports facilities in various U.S. counties. Their research results have been published in the *ISPRS International Journal of Geo-Information*. This paper not only demonstrates the potential of geospatial analysis in solving public health problems but also highlights Professor Mu's professional competence and innovative thinking in applying geographic information systems technology to real-world problems. Through such research, Professor Mu and her team provide new perspectives and methods for improving the uneven distribution of sports facilities, which is of great significance for promoting public health and improving quality of life.

Although this research on sports facility availability was just a small project, it unexpectedly attracted widespread social attention, receiving a response beyond expectations. She humorously remarked that she finally felt she had done something valuable and meaningful for society, breaking free from the limitation of merely "talking on paper." They proposed the concept of **"Play Desert"** (for children and adolescents), calling for improved outdoor activity conditions. Their work was widely reported by more than thirty media outlets including **UGA Today, HealthDay, GPB, and HuffPost**, indicating that her research achievements have not only attracted attention in the academic community but also generated impact at the societal level.

Professor Mu's research group has produced research results with policy value in the public health field. Taking 2023 as an example, in an NIH project she participated in, she guided doctoral student **Zhang Jielu** in studying the spatial distribution and spatial disparities of **Automated External Defibrillators (AEDs)** and exploring how to optimize the spatial layout of AEDs. This type of research is of great significance for improving the efficiency of medical resource allocation. Professor Mu guided doctoral student **Wu Nemin** in collecting social media data to study changes in online shopping behavior for vegetables and daily necessities during the pandemic. Another doctoral student she advised, **Inhye Kong** (currently a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Zurich), published research from her doctoral dissertation period, analyzing the cultural ecosystem of U.S. national parks through social media data.

Professor Mu also pointed out the **blurring of disciplinary boundaries** in modern scientific research, emphasizing that we are moving from a **"discipline-oriented"** era to a **"problem-oriented"** era. She believes that in this context, researchers need to bravely step out of their comfort zones, break disciplinary boundaries, face the most important and cutting-edge issues, and explore using various ideas and methods in order to produce the most impactful work. Professor Mu has been committed to this philosophy in practice, as evidenced by her current interdisciplinary projects exploring **price index issues** with colleagues in agricultural economics, and researching **AI applications** with colleagues in public health and computer science. These works foreshadow that in the future, we can see geographical thinking playing its unique and important role in more fields.

## 03 Growing Together with CPGIS

As the former President of the International Association of Chinese Professionals in Geographic Information Sciences (CPGIS), Professor Mu's growth experience is tightly intertwined with CPGIS. **Professor Gong Peng**, during his teaching tenure at UC Berkeley, did a great deal of work for CPGIS. Mu, who was pursuing her PhD at UC Berkeley, also became CPGIS Secretary-General by a fortunate coincidence. Regarding this history, in the *Tongxin Ji* (Collection of Shared Hearts) published in 2012 to commemorate the 20th anniversary of CPGIS's founding, Mu shared a recollection:

&gt; *"I first heard of CPGIS while studying at UC Berkeley. Many departments at Berkeley are known for their lengthy names, and their abbreviations often cause misunderstandings. For example, the ESPM (Environmental Science, Policy and Management) where Professor Gong Peng was based, often elicited surprised questions: What? ESPN? Berkeley has a sports channel department? Our department, LAEP (Landscape Architecture and Environmental Planning), was also frequently guessed to have some connection with the Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD). At that time, I was the only Chinese student in our program, a bit lonely, while Professor Gong's side had many Chinese students and scholars and was quite lively. So I often ran from the police department to the sports channel. One day, Professor Gong said to me: Mu Lan, do something for CPGIS. My first reaction was, what department is this again? It turned out that Professor Gong's student, Sheng Yongwei, who was then CPGIS Secretary-General, was about to graduate, and Professor Gong wanted me to take over this job. At that time, CPGIS headquarters were located at UC Berkeley, mainly managed by Professor Gong, Pu Ruiliang, and Sheng Yongwei, handling membership registration and journal editing. Our organization was not yet very rigorous and did not have a strict election system. So within one minute, I went from still being bewildered about what CPGIS was, to being on the front lines—not only becoming a member but also becoming Secretary-General. Later, Wang Le also 'was made' Secretary-General. At that time, my impression of Teacher Tu Hanming and Teacher Bao Shuming was their pinyin versions, because I occasionally had email contact with them regarding CPGIS membership affairs but had never met them in person. I found it amusing that the leaders of CPGIS had quite rhyming names."*

In the early stages of her career development, CPGIS provided her with valuable academic resources, exchange opportunities, and professional development support. Therefore, for Professor Mu, CPGIS is not only a professional organization but also a **warm community**. Professor Mu believes that the most important significance of CPGIS lies in providing a platform for exchange and collaboration for GIS scholars from China, enhancing the sense of belonging for Chinese scholars in a foreign land. Over the years, CPGIS has been committed to supporting and nurturing young scholars, organizing numerous activities beneficial to the academic career development of young scholars, such as the **"Go Home"** project organized by Professors Lin Hui and Hu Shixiong, and **Professor Shi Xun's "Health Seminar"**—these activities also had a profound impact on Professor Mu's career.

## 04 Embracing Failure, Respecting One's Inner Self

When sharing her growth experience, Professor Mu has profound insights into the growth of young scholars. The first key point she emphasized is **"respecting one's inner self."** She believes that choosing the path of academic research is not easy, because it not only requires spending many years to obtain a PhD but also involves facing a severe job market and limited income after graduation. Therefore, without **strong interest and passion from the depths of one's heart**, it is difficult to persevere on this path. Professor Mu believes that every researcher must find a field they truly love and are willing to devote their entire life to studying.

Following this principle, when advising students, Professor Mu always **respects students' research interests** and supports their free exploration and development. She believes that pursuing a PhD is a process of **self-discovery**. In the early stages, the advisor may guide the student, but as research deepens, the student should gradually shift toward **self-guidance**, ultimately being able to guide the advisor to some extent. She believes that if a PhD student cannot reach this level upon graduation, their doctoral journey can hardly be considered successful. Throughout the advising process, Professor Mu treats students as **future colleagues and partners**, an attitude that not only reflects her respect for students but also demonstrates her emphasis on academic heritage and collaboration.

Professor Mu also particularly emphasized the importance of **self-recognition and accepting failure**. She believes that encountering failure is an inevitable part of anyone's career. What matters is understanding that **failure does not mean one is not excellent enough**. Here she gave an example of **apple pie**: when a group of people is asked about their personal favorite type of pie, they may choose various flavors such as strawberry pie, blueberry pie, or chocolate pie. However, when this group ultimately must choose only one large pie to share, they will usually choose **apple pie**. This example illustrates that what is ultimately chosen is usually the option considered most universally acceptable, not necessarily any one person's favorite. In a fiercely competitive workplace environment, the ultimate winner is often formed by the **"greatest common divisor"** in a collective decision-making process. But this does not mean that "strawberry pie," "blueberry pie," or "chocolate pie" are not excellent. Therefore, when facing failure, Professor Mu advises that one should not self-doubt—"strawberry pie," "blueberry pie," and "chocolate pie" all have their own shining points. We should bravely embrace our failed selves and continue forward with sincerity and passion.
