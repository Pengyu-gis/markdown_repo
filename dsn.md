## 01 "The COVID Dashboard Boy"

Nowadays, COVID seems to have vanished entirely from our lives. Yet just a few months ago, whenever the TV broadcast the evening news, we would always hear the phrase: "According to statistics from Johns Hopkins University..." But the creator of this world-changing data is, like us, an older alumnus from the GIS field—Dong Ensheng.

When COVID first broke out in 2019, Dong Ensheng had no idea that the pandemic would affect the world for the next three years. At that time, he had just completed the collection of measles data at the U.S. county level with his advisor and was worried about friends in Wuhan. This concern, combined with his professional instincts, made him urgently want to create a platform for updating COVID patient data. Thus, the first version of the COVID-19 Dashboard at Johns Hopkins University (JHU Covid-19 Dashboard) was born.

In Dong Ensheng's mind at the time, the COVID-19 pandemic might just be a small setback for humanity, and this COVID dashboard project was merely an insignificant item among his research projects. However, as the pandemic spread rapidly across the globe, COVID continued to capture the world's attention for the next three years. Dong Ensheng and his platform also became prominent, rising and falling with the waves of news and social media.

When we open the COVID dashboard created by Dong Ensheng, we are all impressed by its simplicity, smoothness, and authoritative data. Yet behind it, Dong Ensheng also encountered countless troubles and difficulties. The first problem came from **data sources**. For the United States, because different states used different statistical standards, data from various states were not consistent in horizontal comparisons. And because countries around the world used different statistical standards, global data did not have absolute comparability. For developing countries, the difficulty of data collection was even greater. Due to incomplete public health infrastructure, developing countries had lower data update frequencies, and accuracy was not necessarily high. Regarding these issues, Dong Ensheng expressed regret that, as data curators, the research team could not directly verify the data and could only give sufficient trust to official statistics.

Additionally, there were **political issues**. For example, Dong Ensheng once received a lawyer's letter from India demanding changes to map boundaries; and there were territorial disputes over regions such as Crimea, Kosovo, and Palestine. Apart from these geopolitical issues, the most common problems were **technical**. Initially, the COVID platform was built on the university's servers. However, as attention increased, the university's servers could not meet the needs of people logging in from around the world. The university's staff rebuilt the underlying system to accommodate more visitors. The production team also held multiple discussions on visualizing pandemic data on maps. Although reflecting data on polygons was more intuitive for viewers, the team ultimately decided to display pandemic data at the geometric center points of regions. First, using points on the map saved a lot of memory; second, it also avoided potential territorial disputes involved in using polygons.

Moreover, due to differences in time zones and data update frequencies among countries worldwide, how to coordinate global data and set the dashboard's update time, and how to aggregate data? These seemingly trivial details in daily life became problems that troubled the production team for a long time when the project was implemented. Fortunately, the team successfully solved these problems and showed us the most perfect result.

The attention brought by COVID also brought Dong Ensheng additional **praise and criticism**. Over the past three years, Dong Ensheng and Johns Hopkins University received praise from all over the world. He became a frequent guest in the news media and a legendary figure on campus, but he also often suffered from online violence. Some countries were unwilling to disclose pandemic data, and some people would attack the production team for other issues, even leading to biased reports from certain media. Fortunately, the university responded to these criticisms one by one.

Due to the significant impact of his research results, JHU's COVID data was not only used by COVID research teams worldwide but also attracted interest from many commercial companies and public sector organizations. Dong Ensheng was teased by others as the **"dashboard boy"** because of this dazzling achievement. For Dong Ensheng, this joke was more like an affirmation of his work—after all, this dashboard boy had truly influenced the whole world!

## 02 Learning Experience

Dong Ensheng's academic journey was not smooth sailing. When he completed his master's degree, Dong Ensheng had obtained a PhD admission opportunity, but due to arrangements between his advisor and himself, he could not immediately enroll in the PhD program. Fortunately, Dong Ensheng eventually found a job smoothly, so he decided to accumulate experience and understanding of American society and the workplace through work.

Initially, Dong Ensheng worked at a power dispatch company in Washington State, USA, doing visualization, generator distribution optimization, and related content. Later, Dong Ensheng worked at multiple companies including Esri, giving him rich practical engineering experience. However, Dong Ensheng still hoped to eventually work in academia, so he applied for a PhD program. Johns Hopkins University's School of Engineering recruits students from diverse backgrounds, with students spanning geography, business, mathematics, biology, public health, and other fields. PhD students with technical and work experience can more smoothly complete their doctoral courses. Being able to apply what they learn to real life and solve industrial problems is very fulfilling.

Regarding the **age anxiety** commonly found among young people in China, Dong Ensheng also shared his views. For the current oversupply in China's job market, many domestic GIS professionals have turned to programming jobs, but there is also the "age 35 hurdle." For China, involution is inevitable; we can only try to gain more experience and prove our value. Regarding the work and life of Chinese students in the United States, Dong Ensheng feels that discrimination in the internet and GIS fields is not very severe, and he has not personally experienced much unpleasantness. Invisible ceilings do exist, which is one of the reasons Chinese people like to change jobs. Dong Ensheng hopes we can adapt to local customs, face injustice with neither arrogance nor servility, and be good at expressing our demands—on one hand, this exercises our abilities, and on the other hand, it helps others better understand our needs.

## 03 GIS and Social Responsibility

GIS always provides infrastructure for other disciplines, which gives GIS a **public welfare attribute**. As the creator of the COVID Dashboard, Dong Ensheng deeply feels this. In one experience attending a cartography conference, the conference focused on discussing how to make maps more suitable for diverse races and genders, respecting different people's diverse ideas. This deeply influenced Dong Ensheng and inspired him to think in the direction of social trends. As GIS students, Dong Ensheng hopes we can look outward and consider the social significance of our projects.

For **entrepreneurship**, because GIS is still often used in government and non-profit institutions, and due to the sensitive nature of geographic information data itself, the large volume of data, the difficulty of data acquisition, and issues involving personal privacy, more often it still requires cooperation with platforms (such as schools, governments, public sector organizations, etc.). Purely commercial GIS may be somewhat difficult. GIS has many public welfare projects, but public welfare does not mean not creating value. Many large companies are paying attention to the public benefits generated by geographic information science, such as Esri, which annually collects some excellent GIS projects currently being carried out by enterprises, which can inspire us GIS students.

## 04 Message

For undergraduates, Dong Ensheng put forward visions in **two aspects**:

1. **For undergraduates studying in the geography direction**, they should be exposed to various directions and strengthen general education. They should have some programming ability and APP development experience, which can improve employability. If they want to pursue academia in the future, they should focus on training their English writing and professional skills; if they want to go to industry, they should list target companies early and do more internships.

2. **GIS professionals should strengthen their sense of social responsibility** and train their ability to think from a spatial perspective, and can use spatial visualization methods such as maps more often for display.
