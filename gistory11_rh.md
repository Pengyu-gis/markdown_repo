# GIStory Issue 11 | Rong Hang (Helena Rong): Boundless Exploration of Technological Innovation and Urban Governance

## Guest Introduction

Rong Hang (Helena Rong) is a PhD candidate in Urbanism at Columbia University. Her main research interests include digital technology, collective intelligence, architecture, and urban planning. During her undergraduate years, she studied urban planning at Cornell University. After completing her bachelor's degree, she continued her studies at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), earning a master's degree in Urbanism.

## 01 Exploration of the Field

### Distributed Systems and Their Application in Urban Management

The core concept of **distributed systems** is to break a system into multiple parts and distribute these parts across multiple independent devices. Such a distributed system means that users have the authority to write and modify their own data, no longer centralizing all programs, functions, and data on a single device, but allowing each individual to own their own data. This stands in sharp contrast to the centralized management computing of large tech companies such as Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter, which store user data on their own servers.

Her research aims to explore the application of distributed databases in urban management, specifically including **fintech**, social institutions such as **voting and social governance**, and **property ownership**. This is a relatively niche field where sociological and theoretical research is relatively uncommon. Therefore, she decided to study the development and evolution of these applications over the past four to five years to discover how these applications differ from traditional centralized management models, using this as a breakthrough point for her research.

Rong Hang discussed in detail the specific applications of distributed technology in urban governance and categorized them into multiple domains. First, she mentioned the application of **identity verification systems (ID systems)**. With the continuous development of artificial intelligence, the question of whether an individual in digital space is human or machine has become particularly important. In the blockchain field, new concepts and applications have actually emerged for this situation, and the contributions of these new technologies are also a focus of attention for distributed urban management technology going forward.

Second, she also discussed applications in **voting and governance**. Traditional election and voting processes usually require manual operation, while online voting is relatively lagging. Blockchain technology can provide new voting mechanisms, combining elements of direct democracy and representative democracy, using tokens to change voting mechanisms and processes, and ensuring transparency and preventing fraud through distributed systems.

Rong Hang also mentioned the application of **public record keeping**. This field requires the storage and authentication of information, which can be applied to public health, land records, supply chains, and other areas.

Furthermore, she discussed the application of **fractional ownership**. This concept is already mature in the art and music fields but is now also being tried in the real estate sector. By turning property into token form, faster transactions can be achieved, allowing more people to participate in wealth accumulation.

Rong Hang also emphasized the impact of **policies and regulations** on technological development, particularly in the blockchain field, where policymakers need to understand new technologies and formulate corresponding regulations. She also pointed out that these innovations require more support and follow-up to succeed in practical applications, and traditional methods and policies also need to adapt to these changes. In summary, blockchain technology is gradually changing traditional urban management methods, but the future remains full of challenges and uncertainties.

### The Social Impact of Distributed Urban Management

From the manager's perspective, some people still harbor concerns about distributed systems, viewing distributed technology as a challenge to centralized management. Because from the perspective of urban management, effectiveness may not be as good as that of a government or certification agency that has a global perspective, can collect everyone's data, and govern the city based on this—so this raises questions about the application of this technology, particularly regarding issues of **data access, modification, and control permissions**. Rong Hang believes:


&gt; **"We use this technology to make existing governance systems more efficient, not to overthrow traditional methods as the ultimate goal."**

Rong Hang emphasized the concepts of distributed computing management and centralized computing management. She believes this is not an absolute opposition, especially in the field of urban governance. Although data may be stored in different locations, the ultimate permissions for data access, modification, and control can also be adjusted. These permissions can be flexibly designed according to the goals and needs of the application and can be managed and decided by government agencies or certification entities. Currently, government-promoted distributed management in cities usually utilizes technologies such as distributed systems as **audit tools**, but the ultimate power to make decisions may still lie with the decision-makers.

So from the citizen's perspective, can we as residents feel the magic of distributed computing management in real life? Rong Hang's answer is yes. She takes **Estonia** as an example. Since 2008, the country has established a blockchain-based management system where citizens' identity information, hospitalization information, and even property purchase records are stored on the system. However, in recent years, people have begun discussing how to let the public participate in these processes to achieve more democratic and transparent information flows. For citizens, they spend most of their time as **users of the system**, not as **designers of the system**, so they may not perceive the existence of blockchain technology because the interface remains unchanged, but the underlying infrastructure has changed. This change allows more citizens to participate in urban governance silently and imperceptibly. Rong Hang mentioned a saying:


&gt; **"Infrastructure is only visible when something goes wrong."**

This emphasizes that when technology operates without failure, even if it is invisible to users, it still profoundly affects people's daily lives, providing convenience for people living in the city. Additionally, Rong Hang mentioned some other attempts, such as the application of distributed systems in voting systems and smart contracts. These attempts aim to achieve more user participation and transparency, demonstrating the possibilities of this technology in other fields. From Rong Hang's perspective, although users may not perceive the differences between distributed and centralized systems, these systems can increase people's participation in urban activities and provide them with more convenient ways to participate in public affairs, including daily payments and voting. These developments bring some interesting changes and opportunities.

*"How might the development of distributed management technology affect our urban management science, or the structure of cities?"* Currently, there are many different views in this field, and people still have many disagreements about the nature of this technology and what effects it should achieve. Some believe this technology can build a completely alternative centralized computing system that replaces relevant management agencies, ultimately liberating humans from urban management; others believe the purpose of this technology is to provide a more efficient order system so that parties can authenticate historical facts or common truths. Rong Hang responded that this impact is still difficult to determine. The ultimate development of technology will be influenced by policies and regulations. For example, in the United States, because Web3-related applications usually involve **tokens**, these tokens can represent valuable assets and can also be used for voting or identity verification. However, there are some unresolved issues in the policy field, such as the legal status and security of tokens. Policymakers usually need time to understand new technologies, so there is a gap between policy and technological development. The future development of technology will be influenced by the scope allowed by policy and the degree to which policymakers understand new technologies. In summary, future development is full of uncertainty because too many factors will influence the development of this field.

## 02 Academic Journey

Rong Hang's experience in choosing her research topic was indeed a journey full of **contingency and uncertainty**. During her undergraduate stage, she majored in architectural design, deeply exploring participatory architectural design and the flexibility and adaptability of urban planning. However, during that period, she had not been exposed to cutting-edge technologies such as blockchain or Web3.

The shift in her research field occurred during her graduate studies at MIT, where she participated in a series of research projects, including topics involving intelligent transportation and architectural design value analysis. This experience not only broadened her horizons but also led her into a world where technology and urban development are closely intertwined, especially in the fields of urban design and architecture.

Entering the doctoral research stage, Rong Hang began to try combining her interest in the Web3 field with her research topics, exploring themes such as **smart cities and the impact of technology on civic participation**, which have many intersections with Web3 application areas. She believes that the cultivation of interest is not smooth sailing; rather, it requires continuously accumulating experience and deep reflection through practice and projects, gradually developing passion and understanding for a specific field.

She emphasized: *"Every experience is an important part of forming interest. Only by connecting these experiences and accumulating enough 'dots' can we see the connections between these dots, thereby transforming interest into a concrete research direction."* For Rong Hang, this is a process requiring constant trial and error and exploration. Although the process may be full of twists and turns, she ultimately believes she will find a field she truly loves.

### Exploring Unknown Territories

Rong Hang also encountered some new fields or situations with no one to guide her when exploring her research direction, but she adopted a series of methods to confirm and continue moving forward. First, she emphasized that even in unfamiliar fields, one can gradually increase understanding of the field through **fundamental research and reflection on problems**, as well as **interaction with industry practitioners**. She suggested participating more in activities and conferences related to the field to build more connections and expand horizons. In this process, she found that her interests and understanding were continuously accumulating and evolving.

Additionally, Rong Hang also mentioned that although the field she now studies is very new, these new paths are all based on **social systems that already exist in our lives**, so we are more often using familiar technologies to solve new phenomena. Rong Hang provided an effective way of thinking—viewing this process of transferring domain knowledge as a process of **continuously building and fine-tuning models**. As long as our research methods are clear, we can delve deeply into the field through appropriate research methods. At the same time, she believes that in the early stages, one may know very little about the field, but through continuous learning and accumulation, one can gradually understand and research more deeply. She actively searches, participates in various activities, and builds connections with new friends, continuously expanding her cognitive scope as part of her research, helping herself better understand and integrate into new fields.

### Combining Research and Practice

Regarding work beyond academia, Rong Hang believes that this combination of research and practice effectively supplements her research. In addition to her academic research work, she also owns her own **design and consulting company**, which collaborates with clients in both the public and private sectors, combining technology, design, and social systems understanding to provide solutions. This industry-academia integration approach allows her to combine theory and practice, providing useful solutions for society and maintaining her identity as a designer. At the same time, she also has an ongoing collaborative project involving the use of **blockchain and smart contract technology** to improve public participation platforms in the planning process. This project incorporates design elements and conducts testing and prototyping in practice to seek new solutions. She stated that she will continue to focus on issues related to **property ownership and community governance**, exploring how to use new technologies to achieve more flexible community governance. This combination of her design background and research interests allows her to build bridges between academia and practical application, continuously developing her research field.

When sharing her experience in achieving these accomplishments, Rong Hang believes that her **design thinking** and understanding of urban design have been somewhat helpful to her research in the blockchain field. Design thinking is not just an abstract concept but a way of thinking that can stimulate creative thinking and open-mindedness. Although not every research project can directly apply design thinking, it can provide an innovative perspective when observing life and thinking about problems, encouraging people to think more creatively. The design field is not just about answering "why" and "how" questions, but about answering **"what if"** questions, which also has some enlightening significance for innovative thinking in other scientific research.

### How to Face and Adjust to Stress

Regarding how to manage stress during her PhD, Rong Hang recalled that in Columbia University's urban planning PhD program, every PhD student is very independent, with no fixed research lab or advisor. This requires PhD students to explore their research directions more autonomously, a process that may bring certain pressure, especially in the initial stage when it is still unclear what to research. However, through this training in independence, she believes this experience has equipped her with the ability to think and research independently. In terms of stress management, Rong Hang mentioned that she also engages in design and consulting work outside her PhD program, which allows her to pursue her interests in the design and consulting fields and switch her mode of thinking, which helps relieve academic pressure. Additionally, she suggested cultivating some **hobbies** to help oneself stay calm and relaxed.

## 03 Message

Rong Hang expressed that she believes no matter which industry or field one conducts research in, there are opportunities for exchange and dialogue with other fields. She encourages PhD students and undergraduate/master's students to communicate more with classmates from different majors and fields, because sometimes these **cross-domain conversations** may spark new inspiration. She believes that the boundaries between various fields are gradually becoming blurred, so cross-domain collaboration and learning are beneficial.
