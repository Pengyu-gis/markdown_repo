# GIStory Issue 17 | Zou Lei: Be a Geographer with Warmth

## Guest Introduction

Zou Lei is a Tenured Associate Professor in the Department of Geography at Texas A&M University and Director of the Geospatial Exploration and Analytics Research (GEAR) Lab. He serves as Vice Chair of the Geospatial Analysis and Modeling Commission of the International Cartographic Association (ICA), Chair of the Geographic Information Science and Systems (GISS) Specialty Group of the American Association of Geographers (AAG), Council Member of the Cartography and Geographic Information Society (CaGIS), Research Committee Member of the University Consortium for Geographic Information Science (UCGIS), Editorial Board Member of the *International Journal of Digital Earth*, Editorial Board Member of *Cartography and Geographic Information Science*, and Thematic Editor and Outreach Team Lead of *Big Earth Data*. His research focuses on responsible geoinformatics, GeoAI, social sensing, digital twins, resilient cities, public health, and environmental health. His research aims to address various challenges in sustainable development through spatial thinking, responsible inquiry, novel spatiotemporal data, intelligent algorithms, and interdisciplinary collaboration. His research has received multiple honors and recognitions, including the 2022 World Geospatial Developers Conference Global Young Scientist Award, the 2025 AAG Spatial Analysis and Modeling Specialty Group Emerging Scholar Award, and the 2025 National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (NASEM) Gulf Research Program Early-Career Research Fellow in Community Resilience and Health.

## 01 Doing Warm Geographic Research: Responsible GIScience

Thank you very much to GIStory for providing this valuable opportunity to share and exchange. With the rapid accumulation of spatiotemporal big data and the continuous evolution of intelligent analysis and modeling technologies, geographic information science has made significant advances in both methodology and application. However, this has also brought many new challenges. Current research heavily relies on multi-source large-scale spatiotemporal data and employs complex algorithms for modeling and analysis, but the data itself often suffers from issues of quality, representativeness, and scale differences, while algorithm design may introduce errors, uncertainties, and opacity, thereby affecting the accuracy and interpretability of research conclusions. These biases trigger broader social and environmental risks in practical applications and decision-making.

To effectively address these challenges and further improve the scientific rigor and practical value of research, our team (GEAR Lab) proposes the research concept of **"Responsible GIScience."** Responsible GIScience emphasizes demonstrating scientific rigor and social responsibility at every stage of data acquisition, algorithmic modeling, result interpretation, and practical application. Specifically: at the data acquisition stage, actively identifying and correcting potential biases in data to ensure sufficient diversity and representativeness; at the algorithmic modeling stage, emphasizing model transparency, reproducibility, and interpretability; at the result interpretation and application stage, systematically assessing the potential impacts of research outcomes on society, the environment, and policy, avoiding negative consequences from misleading conclusions or improper use; furthermore, continuously enhancing the theoretical depth and social value of geographic information science through technological innovation and interdisciplinary collaboration.

This concept is not only an extension of traditional GIScience but also constructs a comprehensive, forward-looking research framework. We advocate being driven by social responsibility, focusing on key social issues, confronting and breaking through the limitations of data and methods, emphasizing the transparency of the research process, the interpretability and transformability of results, and pushing geographic information science toward a direction of greater social value and practical significance. Under the guidance of this concept, my team focuses on innovative spatiotemporal data analysis methods, integrating machine learning, artificial intelligence, and multi-source data modeling to deeply explore the dynamic processes of human behavior and environmental change and their driving mechanisms. At the application level, our lab focuses on researching the spatiotemporal differences in disaster resilience and public health, assessing the impacts of climate, environmental, and social changes on them, and providing scientific support for enhancing urban system adaptability and sustainability.

## 02 The Research Journey — Master's and PhD Application Stage: How to Improve Interview Success Rates?

Every year, many students contact me expressing interest in pursuing master's or PhD studies. This brings me both honor and delight, as well as pressure. I am delighted to see more and more students full of passion for geographic information science. The pressure comes from the fact that among applicants, there are many outstanding students, many of whom are far more excellent than I was as a student. Therefore, I need to continuously improve myself, thinking about how to help them achieve research breakthroughs, so as not to let down their expectations.

Before discussing how to improve interview success rates, I think it is more important to first consider: what kind of advisor suits you? Master's and PhD applications are a two-way process—not only is the advisor "recruiting students," but students are also learning about different advisors' research directions, styles, and team atmospheres, finding the "research partner" who can fight side by side with them, both mentor and friend. I have always felt very fortunate to have met good advisors during my master's and PhD stages who were willing to believe in me, support me, and help me unleash my potential.

Over the past few years, I have recruited 7 PhD students and 3 master's students, and also participated in the department's graduate admissions committee, re-examining master's and PhD applications and interviews from the "judge's seat" perspective. This has given me more thoughts on "how to stand out." So I particularly hope to share these experiences to provide a little reference for students currently applying or planning to pursue graduate studies in the future!

### ·-· Be Fully Prepared, Win the Opportunity ·-·

**(1) Personal Website:** I recommend that students planning to apply for master's or PhD programs create a personal website, even the simplest version. The essence of research is being interested in a scientific question, finding the answer or solution, and sharing this research experience with everyone. I suggest integrating academic background, research/work achievements, and personal skills into your personal website, and pasting the link in your email signature. This not only lets the advisor see your "research resume" at a glance but also conveys a message: "I am confident in my work and willing to share it with everyone."

**(2) Know Yourself and Know Your Advisor:** The key to application is understanding whether a potential advisor's research direction truly aligns with yours. I recommend reading several papers from the team of an advisor you are interested in from the past two years (especially articles where the advisor or their students are first authors) or representative works (such as highly cited or more theoretical articles), to see if the project content and research methods interest you. This way, during online exchanges, you can "readily" discuss overlaps in research interests and "targetedly" share your own views!

### ·-· Actively Showcase Yourself, Maintain Contact ·-·

**(1) Bravely Showcase Yourself:** I have seen some applicants prepare a 5-10 minute PowerPoint presentation for their first meeting with an advisor, introducing their background, research interests, and future plans. I think this is very beneficial. This not only saves time for both parties but also efficiently conveys your research thinking and motivation, and demonstrates your presentation style and skills. At the same time, it allows the advisor to "follow your train of thought" in asking questions, delving deeper into the technical details of the research you are most familiar with.

**(2) Maintain Academic Contact:** Keeping moderate contact with the advisor during the application process is a very important item! Students in my lab regularly emailed me to keep in touch during their applications, such as telling me about recent GRE/TOEFL score updates, which conferences they just attended, new papers published, etc. This lets the potential advisor know that you are continuously progressing and have always been interested in joining their lab.


## 03 The Research Journey — Early Career Faculty Stage: How to Build a Warm Lab?

In my first year of work, I sent out three PhD and one master's admission offers; in the second year, I sent out two more PhD and one master's offers. They all ultimately accepted. For a young faculty member just entering the profession, having so many outstanding students choose to join my team was a tremendous fortune. Due to the pandemic, all five PhD students ultimately enrolled simultaneously in 2021. This meant I had to ensure sufficient, stable research funding to support each student in successfully completing their studies, and also needed to tailor design research directions for them that matched their interests while having innovation and collaborative potential. In the first few years of lab building, I often felt pressed for time, needing to constantly find balance between grant applications, student cultivation, teaching tasks, academic service, and family life. Although the journey was quite arduous, team members gradually got on track. Today, many students have successfully graduated and grown into independent young scholars, becoming master's or PhD advisors, about to welcome their first batch of graduate students at their respective positions. Looking back, I think the following three aspects are important considerations in building a lab.

### ·-· Clarify Research Directions ·-·

From the lab's inception to the present, I have been continuously thinking about two questions: first, why does our lab focus on this research theme (Why this research?), and second, why am I the one to establish this lab (Why me?). The essence of these two questions is finding the intersection of three directions: (1) the direction I am most interested in within the discipline, (2) research questions with long-term development potential, and (3) areas I excel in. This not only relates to the sustainable development of research work but also to whether I can attract a group of like-minded students to together produce more interesting and in-depth achievements and discoveries. In the process of building the lab and research team, I mainly thinking from the following two aspects:

**(1) Building a Research System:** My research focuses on constructing a complete and extensible research system. During my PhD stage, my research revolved around the concept of "resilient cities," introducing novel geospatial big data and spatiotemporal analysis algorithms to quantitatively reveal the spatiotemporal differences in disaster impacts and urban resilience, and on this basis proposing targeted disaster prevention and mitigation tools and response strategies. Early research mainly focused on the relationship between social media data and coastal disasters such as floods and hurricanes. After entering faculty positions, I continuously expanded based on my doctoral dissertation. First, I enriched the types of spatiotemporal data, for example incorporating nighttime light remote sensing, street view images, mobility trajectories, and other diverse data into the resilient cities research framework, and broadened the range of disaster types studied. Second, with the development of artificial intelligence, I began exploring how to use and improve AI technology to extract high-precision, action-oriented information from multi-source big data, such as capturing emergency distress signals from social media data, or automatically identifying building damage degrees from street view images. Finally, I gradually extended the "resilient cities" theoretical framework to the "health resilience" field, incorporating population health into resilience assessment and spatiotemporal analysis, particularly focusing on the compound impacts of environmental pollution, epidemics, and other multiple factors on resident behavior and health, further expanding the theoretical boundaries and practical value of urban resilience research. Reviewing my own research journey, although most work still revolves around resilient cities and spatiotemporal analysis, through continuous innovation and expansion in data types, analysis methods, and practical applications, I realize that there are still many important scientific questions worth deeply exploring in this field.

**(2) Active Academic Exchange:** Although in life I prefer quiet and am relatively introverted, I find that whenever I "muster up courage" to actively participate in academic conferences and exchanges, I always gain unexpected harvests, such as new research ideas, collaborators, or even new research projects. I remember just after receiving the faculty offer from Texas A&M University, before officially starting, I gave a report at a disaster research center on campus, sharing research results from my PhD stage, namely how to combine social media data, natural language processing technology, and spatiotemporal analysis methods to detect the social impacts of natural disasters. Right after the report ended, a professor from the engineering school actively came to exchange, mentioning that my research methods were particularly suitable for the NSF project he was preparing: automatically extracting disaster "micro-data" (i.e., the 5W1H information of disaster events: Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How) from news reports. At that time, when large language models were not yet fully mature, this idea seemed both innovative and full of challenges to me. We hit it off immediately, spending a month together preparing the application, and successfully obtained NSF funding on the first attempt. This was not only my first NSF project after graduating with a PhD but also allowed me to smoothly recruit my first batch of students. Since then, I have become more firmly convinced that sometimes actively stepping out of your comfort zone and participating in academic exchanges is also a "low-risk, high-return" investment.

### ·-· Broadly Recruit Talent ·-·

As mentioned earlier, recruiting students is a two-way selection process. If the team needs members focused on technological innovation, I tend to prefer students who excel in programming and foundational knowledge; if the team has interdisciplinary integration needs, I prefer students with relevant domain backgrounds who are good at discovering research problems. When encountering suitable students, I actively contact them, "appealing to emotion and reason," sincerely inviting them to join the team. Specifically, I usually look for suitable students based on the following key criteria:

**(1) Motivation and Goals:** I highly value whether students have clear motivation and goals, knowing clearly why they want to pursue graduate studies and what their career choices are after completing graduate studies, rather than choosing graduate studies merely out of confusion. I understand that original intentions may change during the educational process, but what I hope to see is that when deciding to pursue graduate studies, they have already done some thinking and have some understanding of graduate life.

**(2) Understanding and Interest:** I hope students have a deep understanding of my future research projects and scientific work, and are genuinely interested in them. When reviewing application materials, I often see vague interest statements such as GeoAI or Big Data. But I prefer students to specifically point out which project or which article interests them and share their views.

**(3) Theory and Technology:** In terms of technology, I expect students to have certain foundations in spatial statistics, programming, big data models, and deep learning. At the same time, I hope they have some understanding of disaster resilience, public health, or environmental health.

**(4) Writing and Confidence:** Research writing experience is an aspect I particularly value. Whether formally published journal papers or course reports, both can help me understand students' writing styles and abilities. Showcasing these writing achievements not only demonstrates students' confidence but is also an important basis for me to evaluate their research capabilities. Therefore, I often encourage interested students to share their writing samples with me.

**(5) Thinking and Communication:** Finally, I emphasize students' active thinking and communication abilities. I hope that during exchanges, students are not merely answering questions or agreeing with my views, but can propose their own insights and even try different methods. In my view, the advisor-student relationship should be a process of mutual exchange and common progress.

**(6) Love for Life:** Every time I have my first online exchange with a student, I ask them this question at the end: "When you temporarily put aside research, what do you like to do most?" I very much hope that everyone in the team can achieve work-life balance, cultivating and maintaining their own hobbies. At the same time, my own experience also tells me that research with relaxation can better ensure physical and mental health. Furthermore, love for life helps us capture social needs, to some extent stimulating new research inspiration.

### ·-· Actively Apply for Projects ·-·

For the long-term healthy and sustainable development of the team, one of my main responsibilities is to provide students with multifaceted support, including stable scholarships, equipment, data, travel support, etc. By ensuring project funding, students can obtain necessary instruments and data, attend conferences and training, helping them grow quickly. Over the past few years, I have also had many explorations in this regard, including the lesson of participating in more than ten project applications in one year but being rejected for all, and the fortunate experience of successfully obtaining funding for a project exceeding one million dollars on my first attempt as Principal Investigator (PI). The following are some personal insights on applying for and managing research projects:

**(1) Clarify Research Strengths:** I think when applying for projects, one needs to clearly recognize their team's research strengths, such as specific technical capabilities, multidisciplinary integration thinking, or excellent communication abilities, and actively and fully demonstrate these strengths in scientific work and academic activities to attract potential collaboration opportunities.

**(2) Choose Suitable Projects:** When selecting or receiving invitations from other teams to apply for funded projects, I recommend first asking yourself one question: if the project application is not approved, will this research direction still be initiated or continued? If the answer is yes, this indicates that the research direction is worth investing in. Conversely, if the project does not align with the lab's long-term research goals, one should adjust the research direction early or decide whether to accept the invitation based on time constraints.

**(3) Build a Reliable Team:** Successful projects depend on team members with rich experience who can contribute unique value. I recommend finding experienced, willing-to-invest-time partners who can complement each other in project division of labor to jointly build the project direction.

**(4) Write Project Proposals:** Writing proposals requires certain accumulated achievements and a deep understanding of project requirements. I recommend deeply reading project solicitation notices or application guidelines, conceiving application content from the reviewer's perspective. When facing projects in new fields, one should quickly grasp key information and adjust proposal content to meet project requirements. For multi-person collaborative proposals, ensure consistent overall writing style before submission so that reviewers can clearly understand project goals and methods.

Learning the structure and content of proposals from experienced advisors is also very critical. During my PhD studies, my advisor Professor Nina Lam gave me a lot of careful guidance on project proposals, laying a solid foundation for my later independent applications for funded projects. When I first entered the PhD stage, Professor Lam did not directly set a clear research direction for me but had me participate in meetings for multiple projects she hosted, giving me ample space to explore my own interests. In this process, I participated most in an NSF-funded project of $1.5 million that she had just launched, mainly using multi-scale spatial analysis and modeling methods to simulate the impacts of climate disasters on the environment and population economy in coastal Louisiana, while assessing the roles of human factors (such as coastal restoration projects, economic transformation, and oil extraction activities) in regional sustainable development. This project was very large, involving close collaboration among five different teams from three different colleges. From enrollment to graduation, I participated in almost every project meeting, closely observing how scholars from different disciplinary backgrounds proposed completely different views and methods around the same scientific problem, how they efficiently communicated and collaborated, and also learning about each advisor's unique style in guiding students. This experience profoundly made me experience the charm and value of interdisciplinary, multi-perspective research.

In my first year of the PhD, Louisiana Sea Grant issued a notice inviting researchers to submit project proposals on community resilience building. After learning this, Professor Lam immediately shared it with team members and proposed that we could develop a GIS platform for disaster resilience assessment to compare, verify, and optimize existing multiple resilience assessment methods. I actively participated in the research methodology part of this project application, designing and producing a series of flowcharts and preliminary research result diagrams. This was my first time participating in formal research project proposal writing. Professor Lam provided very detailed guidance and suggestions on document writing and chart design. Fortunately, we ultimately successfully obtained funding. This news excited the entire team and greatly boosted my confidence. Looking back now at the process of preparing the proposal at that time, I didn't know what a successful project proposal looked like. But perhaps it was precisely because of this "ignorance" that I was able to give free rein to my creativity, with flowchart designs that were more novel and vivid, clearly distinguishing them from traditional resilience assessment methods, thus winning the favor of the judges.

During my PhD proposal stage, I attended the AAG annual meeting and noticed that many studies began using emerging spatiotemporal big data such as social media for urban analysis. But at that time, research mostly focused on understanding human behavior and urban dynamics. Although closely related to urban planning, it rarely involved disaster resilience and emergency response fields. I realized this was a very worthwhile research direction to explore, so together with team members, I proposed this idea to Professor Lam. Professor Lam was very supportive of this proposal and quickly assembled a five-person research team, writing an NSF project proposal within a month. From proposing research ideas, assembling the team, to formulating research plans, determining scientific questions, discussing technical details, and finally collecting materials and submitting the proposal, I was fortunate to fully participate in the entire process. Even more fortunately, this project ultimately successfully obtained funding. Subsequently, based on the research results of this project, Professor Lam's team successively obtained three more NSF project grants, supporting my research during my PhD and postdoc periods. For this, I am always deeply grateful. Reviewing the entire process, actually Professor Lam was initially not very familiar with emerging big data such as social media and mobility trajectories. But it was precisely her sense of responsibility toward students, passion for research, and persistence, learning and exploring from scratch, combined with her rich research experience in disaster resilience, that allowed her to continuously obtain multiple project grants.

All these experiences played a crucial role in my becoming a PI and applying for research projects. For example, a $1.5 million project I obtained in 2023, funded by the National Academies, studies how climate disasters in Texas exacerbate air and water pollution, thereby affecting resident health. This project's design was deeply inspired by my PhD stage experiences in interdisciplinary integration and multi-scale analysis, involving collaboration among four different colleges. Additionally, another project I participated in, funded by NSF with $1.1 million, is dedicated to developing intelligent analysis algorithms, data products, and CyberGIS platforms for different types of geospatial big data to better promote research in the disaster resilience field. The successful launch of this project was precisely based on the disaster resilience theoretical knowledge and data science method training I accumulated during my PhD.

**(5) Efficient Team Collaboration:** As Principal Investigator (PI), one needs to fully prepare and communicate, including assembling the team, designing the project framework, and clarifying project content and individual responsibilities with team members (Co-PIs). I recommend using images and video materials to help team members intuitively understand project goals, thereby reducing avoidable misunderstandings. Every time you have a meeting, you must prepare and share the meeting agenda in advance so everyone is prepared. If you receive an invitation from another PI as a collaborating researcher (Co-PI), I recommend clearly defining your scope of responsibility and required resources, and ensuring tasks are completed on time.

**(6) Maintain a Steady Mindset, Actively Improve:** Project applications often involve uncertainty, and success or failure is influenced by many factors. Every time you submit a proposal full of hope, you should also soberly recognize that not every project will be funded. Therefore, after every proposal submission, I remind myself that even if this project is not funded, I will gain valuable reviewer feedback. This can help me better adjust research content and direction, fill gaps, and prepare for the next application. At the same time, these feedbacks will ultimately help me think more deeply about my own research. For example, one of my NSF projects was not funded on the first submission. At that time, reviewers mentioned that social media data analysis needed to consider data authenticity and user bias, and our proposal did not provide specific solutions to these issues. Therefore, when we submitted the second time, we added algorithms for data authenticity verification and population bias correction, and soon received good news.


## 04 The Research Journey — Advisor Stage: How to Become a Warm Advisor?

On the academic path, encountering a warm and responsible advisor is every student's fortune. Sometimes when scrolling through social media posts about "avoiding" certain advisors, I also read them carefully. As a teacher whose name literally contains "Lei" (thunder), I need to work even harder to make future students "not need to avoid" me.

I am very fortunate to have met many good teachers with both academic aspirations and humanistic care along my educational path. At the same time, during my graduate studies, I was also fortunate to join many warm, united, and loving teams, meeting many senior and junior fellow students who were serious and passionate about research and willing to share sincerely. We had years of fighting side by side in research and many moments enjoying life together. This "comradeship" allows us to continue actively collaborating after graduation. Now, in the role of an advisor, I hope to pass on this warmth and sense of responsibility, striving to become a solid backing for students, hoping that students will have profound attainments in future research fields while also helping them achieve personal growth.

### ·-· Sincere Communication, Teaching According to Aptitude ·-·

I believe the core of education lies in sincere communication. I strive to have open, sincere, and equal communication with students, making them feel relaxed and comfortable in every exchange. Through this relaxed communication, I can deeply understand each student's academic interests and strengths, and tailor research topics for them based on this information, encouraging them to conduct in-depth research in areas they excel in. This personalized guidance not only brings advisor and student closer but also helps students go further on their research path. Sincere communication can also encourage students to propose new ideas, "challenge" traditions, "challenge" some of my research ideas or views, and thereby produce research with distinctive characteristics.

### ·-· Focus on Hard Skills, Enhance Soft Skills ·-·

I focus on cultivating students' hard skills in the geography field, especially professional skills and writing abilities. I believe that showcasing research achievements cannot be separated from solid writing skills. Therefore, when students write their first article, I guide them word by word, helping them clarify logic and improve writing standards. At the same time, I also value students' soft skills, particularly academic communication abilities. I encourage students to use English in group meetings, one-on-one meetings, and email communication, ensuring they can confidently express their research achievements in the international academic circle.

### ·-· Regular Discussions, Gradual Growth ·-·

Weekly individual discussions with students and regular group meetings are important components of my teaching. Through one-on-one exchanges with students, I can help them solve difficulties in research; at the same time, through presentations at group meetings, students not only accumulate experience in academic speaking but also gain an open discussion and mutual learning environment. Additionally, I try my best to design long-term plans for students that run through their entire PhD journey. In the first year, I provide specific guidance to help them adapt to the research environment; in the second year, I encourage students to think independently and design their own research topics; in the third year, I invite students to participate in project management to cultivate management abilities; in the final year, I gradually reduce their workload, allowing them to focus on completing their dissertation and job searching, and preparing for their future careers. Through this orderly cultivation, students gradually form habits of independent thinking and possess solid research capabilities.

Of course, every student has different plans. For example, one of my PhD students, Zhou Bing, expressed his expectation to graduate in three years before enrollment. For this, I designed a different course plan and research pace for him. He ultimately smoothly obtained his PhD in three years and currently joined the University of Tennessee as a Tenure-Track Assistant Professor, about to establish his own lab.

## 05 The Research Journey — Two Families: How to Balance Personal Family and Academic Family?

In modern academia, many professors not only have to cope with busy research tasks but also need to balance family responsibilities. I also face the same challenges, often joking with family and students that I have two "families" to balance—one personal family and one academic family. I also continuously try and adjust, striving through reasonable arrangements, with the understanding and support of family, to achieve balance between academia and family.

### ·-· Mutual Understanding and Support, Reasonable Time Allocation ·-·

I believe that understanding and support among family members is the foundation for balancing work and family. My spouse, Professor Cai Heng, is also a professor at TAMU. We cope with busy work and family responsibilities through mutual cooperation. When one party is particularly busy with work, the other takes on more family affairs. I pay more attention to reasonable time allocation to improve work efficiency. For example, I use efficient time periods for research and writing, and arrange administrative work during lower-energy periods. Sometimes, I use fragmented time for physical exercise or handling small tasks. For example, when taking children skating or playing soccer, I also exercise, or when taking children to tutoring classes, I process emails and update the lab website. I think sometimes sacrificing a certain amount of work time for the health and happiness of family is necessary and worthwhile. For me personally, family happiness is the value of all efforts.

### ·-· Focus on Core Areas, Optimize Research Strategy ·-·

Human energy is limited. Sometimes, trying to兼顾 all tasks and making every piece of work perfect often comes with sacrifices to health and family. Therefore, when facing busy research tasks and numerous collaborations, I selectively decline some work not closely related to my long-term research direction and recommend more suitable colleagues. This way, I can focus on the research areas I am most interested in. For those administrative or repetitive tasks, I focus on efficiency and final practical effects, trying to streamline every step, aiming to complete rather than overly pursuing perfection.

In modern academia, many professors not only face busy research tasks but also need to balance family responsibilities. Dr. Zou is precisely such a mentor who can find balance between academia and family. His experiences and thoughts believe they can provide valuable reference for many young scholars.
