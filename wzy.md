# GIStory Issue 8 | Wang Zhangyu: Diverse Backgrounds Build a Broad Horizon for GIS

## Guest Introduction

Wang Zhangyu is a PhD candidate in the Department of Geography at the University of California, Santa Barbara (UC Santa Barbara). His primary research interests lie in the application of machine learning and artificial intelligence algorithms in urban spatial analysis. He previously earned his bachelor's degree from the Department of Architecture at Tsinghua University and his master's degree from the Department of Information and Computer Sciences at the University of Massachusetts Amherst (UMass-Amherst).

## 01 On the Opportunities and Challenges of Pursuing a PhD

Currently enrolled in the PhD program in the Department of Geography at UC Santa Barbara, Wang Zhangyu is a young scholar full of passion and exploratory spirit. His doctoral journey carries unlimited potential and hope. Looking back, from the difficult choice he faced after completing his master's degree to now having entered the realm of doctoral research, every step has been filled with courage and perseverance.

**"Continue with a PhD or go to work?"** This was the crucial decision Wang faced after graduating from the UMass CICS master's program.

Three years ago, he resolutely chose to pursue a PhD. In the choice between a doctorate and work, he was acutely aware of the pressure that time brings, and that the decline of age and capability would undoubtedly affect the choices one could make at some point in the future. Wang's decision was to try a PhD, unafraid of age and challenges. If one could find one or several points of interest in the vast sea of human knowledge, it would be a wonderful thing to start a PhD at what you consider the most suitable time.

Whether to choose a PhD depends largely on personal experience and expectations. If someone is naturally disinclined toward research and writing, they will gradually distance themselves from the PhD path starting from undergraduate studies. Choosing a PhD, whether to continue research or apply what one has learned in industry, requires **intrinsic motivation**—a curiosity about the unknown world. On the other hand, some students might avoid a PhD because they do not want to write papers, since writing papers is not something that comes automatically from doing good experiments; good experiments do not guarantee good papers, nor do they guarantee publication on sufficiently influential platforms. Often, conducting experiments and writing papers is repetitive, painstaking work—otherwise, there would not be so many paper-editing services.

In summary, Wang believes that a PhD life centered on research he is interested in is worthwhile for him. Although he has struggled with paper writing, his interest and curiosity have always driven him to keep moving forward.

## 02 The Experience of Doing GIS and AI from an Interdisciplinary Perspective

Before the interview, we learned that Wang Zhangyu's undergraduate studies were in architecture and planning, after which he chose to pivot toward algorithm development and application. So, what kind of experiences and feelings has this interdisciplinary journey brought him? And in the direction of GIS combined with AI, do researchers with diverse disciplinary backgrounds possess certain advantages?

&gt; **"Sometimes the research direction may be the same, but when people have different professional backgrounds, they see different things."**

Wang believes this question is very important—the impact of an interdisciplinary background is even greater than he initially imagined. People with an urban planning background can help improve models and make problem-solving methods more aligned with the practical problems we want to solve. When we apply appropriate algorithms to different fields, we find that these algorithms usually claim to be general-purpose and applicable under various conditions. However, if we directly apply general models trained on large datasets to specific domains like remote sensing, we may encounter many problems.

For example, remote sensing photos typically contain multiple channels, and determining the resolution of photos is often a challenge. If we are using satellite photos, their resolution is higher than the conventional 512×512 pixel models. At the same time, we need to consider which channel to process and to what extent. For instance, objects like trees may have mutual occlusion that could affect processing results. Therefore, if we want models to function properly under these conditions, relying on pre-trained algorithms suitable for other scenarios may be insufficient. We will likely need to manually label some data or perform feature extraction. For example, we can utilize features such as tree growth patterns to transform general models previously used for face recognition or car detection into models suitable for tree detection. This work may require building our own datasets and adding additional features on top of existing simple models.

This also raises the question of researchers' expectations for results and the purpose of analysis. This is a critical factor. If a researcher simply takes a method and looks for a solution to apply it to, they may at some point discover that the method is completely unsuitable. But to complete a paper, we might still insist on using it, and the final result may appear forced and unnatural. Therefore, it is important to understand our expectations for results and conduct analysis accordingly—we cannot rigidly apply methods merely to accommodate paper writing.

Wang Zhangyu also shared with us some aspects he has found worthy of in-depth research:

For example, when processing geographic information, we must consider not only the relationships between coordinates but also the geographic structures corresponding to objects of different shapes. Through analysis of object shapes, we can classify and predict more accurately, thereby improving model performance. In current research, the structured consideration of spatial information remains insufficient—for instance, when using graph structures, it may be impossible to express complex spatial relationships. Therefore, researching more complex spatial structures may be a new direction for the future.

On the other hand, from an information theory perspective, we cannot yet generalize whether tabular or image data formats better preserve information. The key to judging data quality and research value lies in the **signal-to-noise ratio** of the data that fills them. Tables extracted by experts may contain higher-quality geographic information, while the challenge with images lies in identifying useful information from noise. Using transfer learning or feature extraction methods can indeed facilitate research on geographic information, but specific implementation must consider data quality and special training requirements. In processing geographic information, some methods tend to use general models to extract features first, then use regression models to identify which features are useful. If a model specifically extracts the required features, no additional steps are needed. This is a good idea, but how to specifically implement it requires further discussion.

As we deeply explore the world of interdisciplinary research, as Wang Zhangyu shared, we will discover the intersection points between various disciplines, providing more possibilities for solving complex real-world problems. From urban planning to algorithm development, from image processing to geographic information and spatial relationships, the convergence of these fields will spark innovation and broaden research horizons. At the same time, interdisciplinary research also requires researchers to possess more background knowledge and skills in order to have more opportunities to explore unknown territories.

## 03 Does a PhD Really Have Work-Life Balance?

Pursuing a PhD is a complex and **personalized** experience; there is no one-size-fits-all Work-Life Balance model that applies to everyone. Perhaps the most practical solution is to continuously evaluate and adjust throughout the doctoral journey to find a balance between work and life that suits oneself. Different people will have different views and experiences, which is one aspect that makes PhD life colorful, but it also requires careful consideration and self-management. When answering questions about Work-Life Balance, Wang proposed an interesting perspective:

&gt; **"If work and life in a PhD are inseparable, then have we internalized this problem?"**

This viewpoint provides profound food for thought, forcing us to re-examine the essence of PhD life. If work and life in a PhD seem inseparable, does that mean we have internalized this issue, treating it as an unavoidable state? Some might advocate viewing a PhD as a lifestyle, full of passion and dedication, rather than merely a job. They believe that loving one's research project and devoting oneself wholeheartedly to it can produce more outstanding research results.

However, on the other hand, we must also recognize the stress and anxiety that excessive work devotion may bring. PhD life may be accompanied by relentless deadlines, high-intensity work, and a competitive environment, which can negatively impact our health and well-being. Therefore, while pursuing a doctorate, we need to examine our motivations and expectations. Are we truly willing to make PhD life an inseparable way of being, or should we place more emphasis on Work-Life Balance, ensuring that work and life maintain some degree of balance?

There is no standard answer to this question, because everyone's situation is different. Some may find a perfect balance between work and life, while others may prefer to view their PhD as part of life. The key is that we should consciously weigh our options and decide how to spend this doctoral journey according to our own needs and values. Ultimately, our goal should be to live a happy, meaningful life while achieving success in academia—and this requires us to constantly think about and adjust our balance between work and life.

## 04 Message

At the end of the interview, we also asked Wang Zhangyu to share some advice and encouragement for students studying GIS.

&gt; **"The field of GIS combined with computer and data science is not merely a stacking of models and algorithms. What matters more is whether it can bring new knowledge to science, whether it can explain existing theories, or even whether it can explore new theories. This part of the journey may be arduous, but it is precisely in this exploration that true breakthroughs and innovations occur."**

For those who have already chosen this field, especially students currently pursuing a PhD, **persist and believe in your decision**. Interdisciplinary research is a challenging field where **failure is the norm**, but it is also full of opportunities. Through continuous exploration, you will gain a deeper understanding of the world, find new ways to solve complex problems, and contribute to the progress of science and society. Regardless of your disciplinary background, you can bravely embark on the journey of interdisciplinary research to discover the wonders of unknown territories. **Persevere—setbacks are not the end, but the inevitable path to success.** In this journey of pursuing knowledge and innovation, you will continue to grow, contribute your strength to broader social and scientific causes, and move toward a broader horizon.
