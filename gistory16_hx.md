## 02 Seeking Truth on Hot Soil

To truly ground research in reality, one must step out of the laboratory and into the front lines. To advance a malaria prevention and control project in collaboration with the Gates Foundation, Dr. Huang Xiao led his team to the land of Ethiopia, launching a truly "hardcore field investigation."

In Africa, research is not about typing code in front of a computer but departing at 5:30 AM, carrying equipment and hiking several kilometers, traversing highlands, swamps, and valleys in 40-degree heat to collect adult and larval data of Anopheles stephensi mosquitoes. There, research is more like a dual test of body and will.

They stayed in humble hotels where beds were made of a few wooden planks, relying solely on mosquito nets for protection. At night, the barking of wild dogs and buzzing of mosquitoes rose and fell in succession. Along the way, they saw children walking three kilometers to school every day and witnessed local people's strong desire for public health resources. During the drone project advancement, the research team also communicated and negotiated multiple times with local military authorities—in the Afar region, flight permits were far more than a simple application; they were games of strategy and rounds of trust-building.

Research cannot be done behind closed doors; it must go out, be spoken about, and be seen. To truly make research impactful, one must actively share it, letting more people understand what you are doing and what you care about.

Dr. Huang Xiao deeply understands this. He frequently updates his academic dynamics on LinkedIn, and as of May 2025, has accumulated over **6,300 followers**. In his view, academic promotion is not only about showcasing results but also an effective way to connect. By sharing research progress, one can not only gain feedback and resonance but may also spark new collaboration inspirations, stimulating more potential possibilities.

&gt; *"The starting point of anything is from 0 to 1."* Dr. Huang said. Even if there aren't many followers at first, as long as you continuously output and share content rhythmically, influence will gradually accumulate.

## 04 Job Hunting Is Not Just About Luck

On the path of academic job hunting, no one can succeed with a single application. Facing fierce competition and high uncertainty, actively trying and persistently applying are the keys to increasing your hit rate. Dr. Huang Xiao's experience can be summarized in six words: **Apply! Apply! Apply!**

Today's academic market is more competitive than ever. On one hand, there are more and more applicants; your competitors may be peers with PhDs, scholars who already hold faculty positions, or even your own advisors or collaborating professors. On the other hand, there are not many truly open positions; some positions are "locked in" early by senior scholars, leaving limited remaining opportunities. Therefore, fighting a protracted war and applying to enough positions is the only way to break through.

In terms of school selection, Dr. Huang suggests adopting a **"gradient strategy"**—don't just stare at top-tier schools, but also cover mid-tier institutions and regional universities. Opportunities in the academic market are dynamically changing; sometimes competition at top schools is extremely fierce, while sometimes you might unexpectedly find openings in unimaginable places. If you only target a few "dream schools," you may miss opportunities due to fluctuations. It's better to set a reasonable goal: apply to **20-30 schools**, and you will eventually encounter the offer that belongs to you.

Of course, applying to many does not mean "applying blindly." To truly seize opportunities, you must do your homework thoroughly.

Dr. Huang emphasizes that **carefully reading job announcements and meticulously polishing application materials** is a decisive step. When you see a suitable position, read the JD (job description) several times, catch the keywords, and precisely align your research content, teaching experience, and position requirements. When writing materials, it's best to combine them with the target college's curriculum and training programs, letting the other party see what you can bring to them, rather than sending generic "I'm excellent, please give me a chance" templates.

Job hunting is both screening and showcasing. A carefully prepared application letter is often more touching than a hundred template-style materials. On this track where you compete with both strength and preparation, those who are truly prepared are more likely to seize important opportunities.


## 05 Students Are Also Partners

Dr. Huang Xiao often says: *"Curvature doesn't matter, slope does."* In other words, the starting point is not important; the speed of growth is what matters. When selecting students, what he values most is not past transcripts but students' **growth potential, research interests, and expectations for research**.

During the admissions process, Dr. Huang does not limit himself to "hard metrics" in application materials. Compared to paper counts or background pedigree, he cares more about students' comprehensive qualities and passion for research. He conducts multi-round screenings, eventually engaging in in-depth interview exchanges with **10-20 students**, understanding their personalities, goals, and how they view research. Additionally, he contacts recommenders to evaluate students' potential from multiple angles.

Of course, resumes and personal statements are still important references. There have been students with no papers on their resumes but who demonstrated outstanding project experience, skill growth, and exploratory spirit, equally moving him. Dr. Huang is always willing to open doors of opportunity for students with potential and passion.

Dr. Huang also shared a special case: a master's applicant had long interacted with him through LinkedIn before applying—liking, commenting, and even sending emails to discuss research ideas. Day after day of proactive communication left a deep impression on Dr. Huang. Later, he thought: *"Why not just have him come to the U.S. and chat in person!"* So, this student received a master's offer. Sometimes, true opportunities lie hidden in sincere interactions again and again.

## 06 PhD Advancement Complete Guide

In the early stages of an academic career, many PhD students encounter such confusions: how to choose a research direction? How to cultivate systematic research thinking? How to accumulate connections? These questions may seem complex, but they all have rules to follow. In the interview, Dr. Huang Xiao shared his experience, providing some practical advice for students currently in the PhD stage.

**Step one: clarify what kind of researcher you want to become.** Everyone's growth background and interests differ, and there is no fixed template for the academic path. Dr. Huang Xiao believes that everyone will eventually forge their own path, and only they can decide this path. If you are skilled in technology, you can delve into algorithms and models; if interested in practical applications, you can develop toward applied research; if you prefer humanities, social sciences, and critical thinking, human geography or social science directions are also excellent choices. The premise for all this is having a clear understanding of yourself, knowing what you truly want to do, rather than drifting with the tide. Development trajectories vary from person to person; the key is finding the direction most suitable for yourself.

**Step two: the cultivation of research thinking is a natural process.** Many PhD students worry about lacking a big-picture perspective or the ability to think systematically. Dr. Huang Xiao candidly stated that he had similar confusions during his PhD, focusing more on specific research details at the time rather than thinking from a "big picture" perspective. But as experience accumulates, we continuously trace more causality from every small problem before our eyes, undergoing constant polishing in this process; this systematic thinking mode will naturally form gradually. Especially after we graduate with a PhD and become independent researchers in academic positions, our accumulation in this area will be more rapid and comprehensive. Therefore, there is no need to over-worry; what matters is maintaining an attitude of learning. As research deepens, research thinking will naturally be exercised.

**Step three: academic conferences are an "efficient social venue" for PhD students to accumulate connections.** Doing good research is certainly important, but in academic circles, knowing peers and building connections is equally key to success. Dr. Huang Xiao shared that during his PhD, he met many outstanding professors and students through academic conferences, many of whom later became his collaborators. So, don't just treat academic conferences as places to give presentations; treat them as social platforms, actively communicate, expand your network, and let yourself enter larger academic circles.

Finding the right direction for yourself, elevating research thinking, and actively expanding connections will allow you to go further on the academic path.
