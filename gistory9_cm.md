Chen Min is a Professor and PhD Advisor at the School of Geography, Nanjing Normal University (NNU), Deputy Director of the Key Laboratory of Virtual Geographic Environment of the Ministry of Education, recipient of the National Science Fund for Distinguished Young Scholars, and Jiangsu Distinguished Professor. He currently serves as President of the International Association of Chinese Professionals in Geographic Information Sciences (CPGIS) for 2024–2025, Vice President of the International Environmental Modelling and Software Society (iEMSs), Chair of the Geographic Information Modelling Commission of the International Geographical Union (IGU), Chair of the Young Innovators Network of the International Society for Digital Earth (ISDE), and Chair of the Working Committee of Young Geographers of the Asian Geographical Association (AGA). He primarily engages in research on geographic modelling and simulation, and has led the development of the Open Geographic Modelling and Simulation Platform (OpenGMS), establishing international discourse power for Chinese research teams. He is the first Chinese Fellow of the International Environmental Modelling and Software Society and a Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society. He has received the Special Prize of the China Geographic Information Science and Technology Progress Award, the First and Second Prizes of the Ministry of Education Natural Science Award, the Austrian Academy of Sciences Tobler Young Researcher Award, the Jiangsu Province Young Science and Technology Award (as one of the "Top Ten Young Science and Technology Stars of Jiangsu"), the Young Scientist Award of the Geographical Society of China, and the Natural Resources Science and Technology Progress Award (Young Scientist Award). His achievements were selected among the Top Ten Research Advances in Chinese Geography in 2021. He serves as Executive Editor-in-Chief of *Annals of GIS* and Co-Editor-in-Chief of *Environmental Modelling & Software*.

## 01 The Academic Journey at Nanjing Normal University

Professor Chen Min's academic journey began with his undergraduate major—**mathematics**. In 2004, one-third of the 60 students in his class were preparing for graduate school entrance exams, and he was among them. However, while the vast majority continued to choose mathematics for further study, Chen decided to forge a different path. He recalled: *"During my undergraduate years, the tedious formula derivations and pure theoretical learning in mathematics often seemed dry, but if I could find a breakthrough for practical application, it would be very attractive."* With this idea in mind, he began to seriously research interdisciplinary and cross-institutional options for graduate school. Considering that NNU's GIS program already possessed considerable influence at the time, he finally made a bold decision to switch majors and become a GIS master's student at his own university.

*"Later, those in our class who succeeded in the graduate entrance exams basically stayed in mathematics—only I alone transferred to GIS."* Chen reflected.

Chen candidly admitted that he was "quite pragmatic" when choosing his graduate program. Because he was still unfamiliar with it, he had no special interest or sentiment toward geographic information systems. *"At that time, I chose GIS not because I deliberately pursued geographical research, but simply because it ranked higher at NNU and had better employment prospects."* However, fate sometimes seems predestined. Chen's master's advisor—Professor Lu Guonian—became the guide who led him onto his newly chosen academic path. This towering figure in academia took this student who was still somewhat green in GIS and brought him into the magnificent palace of geography, also pointing the way for his future doctoral research. For Chen, Professor Lu was not only his mentor but also the shaper of his academic thinking. He recalled: *"Professor Lu always encouraged us to think deeply about what GIS means to geography, and what unique strengths NNU's GIS has compared to remote sensing and surveying disciplines at Wuhan University and Beijing Normal University."* This guidance and inspiration gradually allowed Chen to appreciate the charm of geography. *"Under Professor Lu's influence, I gradually developed a passion for geography and became a true geoscientist."*

## 02 Postdoctoral Training at CUHK

In 2009, hoping to *"go out and see a bigger world"*, Chen discussed with Professor Lu and decided to go to The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK) to follow Professor Lin Hui, a pioneer in the field of virtual geographic environments. In fact, the friendship between Professors Lin Hui and Lu Guonian dates back to 1991. At that time, the two discussed research on virtual geographic environments beside Niagara Falls, but due to technological limitations at the time, the project was not immediately launched. By 2004, under Professor Lu Guonian's leadership, NNU established the Key Laboratory of Virtual Geographic Environment of the Ministry of Education. For Chen, the Space Institute established by Professor Lin Hui at CUHK became his first choice for deepening his research. Although Wendy Guan, Director of the Harvard University GIS Service Center, also extended an invitation to him, Chen firmly chose the Space Institute at CUHK, which was more compatible with him.

However, choosing this path was not smooth sailing. At first, Professor Lin Hui was not particularly optimistic about a PhD graduate from NNU. *"The postdoctoral selection there was very strict, often preferring researchers from top-tier institutions."* Chen explained. But with Professor Lu Guonian's strong recommendation and Chen's own persistence and hard work, he ultimately won Professor Lin Hui's trust, becoming the first postdoctoral researcher from NNU at the Space Institute.

*"Not letting my advisor lose face was my motivation to push myself."* Chen recalled. *"Being able to do a postdoc at CUHK was a huge turning point in my life."*

His work experience at CUHK, especially Professor Lin Hui's guidance, had a profound impact on Chen. When studying at NNU, the emphasis was more on system development, with less attention paid to publications, so Chen had not published a single SCI paper before coming to Hong Kong. But over time, Chen gradually realized that academic research was not just pure experimentation and practice—publishing SCI papers, collaborating with international teams, and participating in international academic organizations and conferences were equally important. Fortunately, Professor Lin Hui delegated some academic conference organization work to the postdocs at the institute, requiring them to independently take charge of inviting relevant professors and communicating with them. This provided Chen with valuable opportunities to gradually cultivate his ability to communicate and engage with the international academic community.

Reflecting on his academic path, Chen said with emotion: *"I learned unique skills from Professors Lu Guonian and Lin Hui respectively. In academic research, I began to have clear goals; in international exchange, I also accumulated rich experience. It is what I learned from them that allowed me to synthesize and integrate, making myself continuously progress and become better."*

Chen's experience fully proves that one's starting point does not determine their future. What is decisive is **firm determination, continuous effort, and suitable opportunities**. Although he was not highly regarded when he first joined the team, he did not mind and instead became even more diligent. Chen said: *"There was a lot to do at the institute—important and unimportant things—every day was full. But for me, every small task was a learning opportunity."* It was precisely because of this persistence and earnestness that Chen was able to complete a challenging 863 sub-project on his own in a short time, earning high praise from Professor Lin Hui. Chen stated, *"Intelligence is certainly important, but what work requires more is persistent effort."* At the same time, his sincerity and humility won him the closeness and trust of the team, and soon everyone called him **"Second Senior Brother" (Er Shixiong)**. This title not only represented his status at the institute but also reflected the deep emotional bonds between him and team members. *"Working hand in hand with my fellow students on research is a refreshing experience."* Chen said. *"This may also benefit from the team spirit that Professor Lin Hui has always advocated—he has always silently devoted himself behind the scenes, leading us toward success."*

Chen's academic journey was full of hardships and challenges. For many researchers, rejection by top journals can be a frustrating blow, but Chen's attitude toward this was completely different. In 2013, after publishing an article on virtual geographic environments in the *Annals of the AAG* and gaining industry recognition, Chen set his sights on another more challenging target: *Earth-Science Reviews*, which was ranked among the top three journals in the geosciences at the time. But shortly after submission, Chen quickly received a rejection letter, with the reason being that the content was not relevant to the journal. Chen did not choose to retreat. On the contrary, he firmly believed that GIS belongs to earth sciences, so he resolutely wrote to the journal editor, striving to secure a fair evaluation opportunity for his research. After multiple communications with the journal, his persistence finally received a response. The journal not only agreed to send his paper for review but also specially hired an editor for GIS and remote sensing articles. Eventually, Chen's article was successfully published. This experience was also an important turning point in Chen's academic career, making him rise to prominence in the field of virtual geographic environment research. In academia, besides profound scholarly attainment, one also needs **firm belief, patience, and perseverance**.

Another important matter also demonstrated the value of persistence. From 2012 to 2014, Professor Lin Hui, as the Chief Scientist, led the application for the national major basic research project "Simulation and Assessment of the Interaction Between Global Change and Human Activities." The application process was full of hardships, and as the project secretary, Chen's resilience and perseverance played an important role. The first two applications for the project were not approved due to various reasons. For the third attempt, some collaborating professors had become relatively pessimistic, leaving only Chen to polish the application alone. After submitting the application, Brown University offered Chen a two-month free visiting scholar opportunity, but considering that the project might require a review defense, he chose to postpone it. Although Professor Lin Hui also advised him to seize this rare visiting opportunity, Chen firmly stated that he would rather give up the Brown visiting opportunity and wait for the preliminary review results. As if arranged by fate, the project indeed smoothly passed the first round of evaluation and entered the defense stage. After a carefully prepared defense, the project was ultimately successfully funded. Chen summarized his decision this way: *"Life always presents us with various opportunities, but we must understand what we truly want. Only by choosing opportunities that suit us and are truly valuable can we go further."*

## 03 The Birth and Development of OpenGMS

When Chen joined the Key Laboratory of Virtual Geographic Environment of the Ministry of Education in 2004, his thinking was the same as most people's—virtual geographic environments were systems like digital cities or virtual environments. But as his research deepened, he realized that a virtual geographic environment is not merely a static environment; it should be a system that dynamically corresponds to the real geographical world. This means one cannot be satisfied with superficial visualization but must delve deeper into the evolutionary processes and mechanisms behind it. Therefore, Chen decided to shift his research focus from 3D visualization to deeper-level **mechanism process modelling and simulation**, and began promoting related research during his doctoral studies.

In 2015, Chen returned to Nanjing Normal University and worked with his advisor, Professor Lu Guonian, to jointly advance this innovative project. He emphasized: *"In fields such as soil, atmosphere, and hydrology, geography already has many specialized models. As GIS researchers, we are not trying to redevelop these models, but to build a unified platform that supports the use and integration of these models."* In Chen's view, the greatest value of a platform built by GIS lies in its ability to provide **sharing functions** for various specialized models, thereby achieving efficient resource integration and utilization, and promoting true cross-domain collaboration among experts in various fields on the platform. In 2017, the **Open Geographic Modelling and Simulation Platform (OpenGMS)** officially began providing services.

When asked about his vision and expectations for OpenGMS, Chen, with fervent hope for the future, wished that researchers from both domestic and international backgrounds could use this platform to jointly promote scientific and technological progress. In January 2021, Chen and his team joined forces with multiple domestic academic organizations to jointly call for **"Developing Domestic Geographic Analysis Models and Building an Autonomous Model System Ecosystem."** Chen expressed with deep feeling: *"In our country, related research often tends to use foreign models, or assumes that developing models is not as good as directly writing applied research articles. But we hope OpenGMS can inspire more researchers to devote themselves to the development of domestic models, forming an autonomous model ecology, and pushing our own innovative achievements to the world to benefit more people."*

To achieve this goal, since 2021, Nanjing Normal University, together with the Geographical Society of China, the China Society of Natural Resources, the China Association of Geospatial Information, and the Chinese National Committee for the International Society for Digital Earth, has jointly organized the **"Domestic Geographic Analysis Model Training Workshop"**. Over three years, it has supported nearly **5,000 teachers and students** from over **700 universities and institutions** in more than **10 countries** to learn models developed by Chinese researchers themselves online for free. Chen's hope is to promote the internationalization and sharing of knowledge, further advancing the comprehensive development of the geography discipline. He pointed out: *"We can develop a model on our own and use it to publish an article, but this merely satisfies personal needs. From the perspective of scientific research, a new model being used by more people is the true way to enhance research efficiency."*

Chen recalled that when they began developing the new platform in 2008–2009, people were not very receptive to the concept of open modelling. But by 2017–2018, as researchers increasingly recognized the value of open science, there was a growing consensus that the more open models and methods are, the more they can be utilized by the broad research community, thereby enjoying the benefits brought by innovative models. Chen emphasized: *"We hope that through this platform, everyone can truly trust and use the latest models developed by researchers in various fields, thereby promoting interdisciplinary collaboration and pushing geographical research to a higher level."*

OpenGMS indeed experienced many difficulties and cold shoulders in its early days, which is a challenge that many new technologies and research achievements face at the outset. Building a platform often requires several years or even longer, and the same is true for models. Thus, Chen adopted a **"war sustains war"** strategy, providing a solid financial foundation for OpenGMS's construction. However, for many innovative products launched by non-top-tier universities, it is initially difficult to gain broad recognition in the domestic academic community. Chen's strategy was **"saving the nation through a circuitous route"**—first accumulating reputation abroad before turning to the domestic market. This could not only effectively circumvent some inherent prejudices but also ensure that OpenGMS truly meets international standards, giving it both international influence and broad domestic application.

Since 2017, the OpenGMS team has begun to make its mark in the international academic community. Their active participation, high-level research achievements, and the platform's practicality have earned them high recognition internationally. This international recognition won them respect domestically and ultimately garnered numerous honors. Chen's experience tells us that scientific research is not just about sitting in a laboratory completing experiments and studies one by one; it also requires **vision and strategy**—the ability to see the greater value behind research achievements and to promote and apply them through appropriate means, thereby allowing more people to benefit.

## 04 The Polygonal Warrior in Teaching and Research

<p align="center">

&gt; **In 2021, Chen Min received the Jiangsu Province Young Science and Technology Award and was named one of the "Top Ten Young Science and Technology Stars of Jiangsu"**

The atmosphere in Chen's laboratory is remarkably harmonious, renowned throughout the school and even the entire university. This is inseparable from his outstanding ability to lead teams and cultivate students. **Team culture** is something Chen places great importance on, dating back to his postdoctoral days in Hong Kong. Professor Lin Hui would organize an annual gala for the Space Institute to strengthen team communication and build team culture. It was in such an environment with a strong sense of team belonging that Chen spent his postdoctoral years. Returning to NNU, Chen continued Professor Lin Hui's tradition of team building, ingeniously establishing a **group uniform conferral ceremony** for new students joining the lab. Specially customized T-shirts and jackets gave new lab members their initial sense of identity.

Chen also makes full use of lab resources to motivate students' enthusiasm for research. Besides weekly large group meetings, Chen arranges subgroup meetings by research direction and invites top international scholars to participate in small group discussions, reviewing research progress and achievements. *"As long as I can reach professional-related teachers, I will invite them to our group meetings. Through exchanges with these excellent mentors, students can gain valuable professional advice, which further motivates them to conduct follow-up research."* Chen said proudly. *"My own knowledge is, after all, limited. It is impossible to fully understand all cutting-edge technologies and developments in every direction. But by inviting teachers with complementary strengths, students can comprehensively absorb more knowledge, which is very important for their future scientific research."*

*"Not only publish high-quality papers for oneself, but also guide students to reach the same standard!"* Chen demonstrated rich experience in cultivating students. *"First, teachers need to have strong research passion. Through continuous encouragement, ignite students' research fire. When they have opportunities to exchange and discuss research with top scholars in international fields, their motivation to write papers will naturally increase. As a mentor, one must not only delve deeply into research oneself but also inspire students to embrace enthusiasm and strive for progress. In this way, the entire team will form a virtuous cycle: the team sees opportunities, members are willing to put in effort, the mentor integrates resources, keeping the whole team continuously confident in publishing high-quality research achievements."*

Chen continued: *"Initially, I did not think too much about paper outputs; I just hoped to first improve the platform's construction. Later, I gradually realized that substantial paper outputs were needed to support this platform. Previously, I thought publishing in Earth-Science Reviews was already a huge success. But it was not until 2020 that I began planning to have the team aim for higher-impact journals such as Nature sub-journals. It is not necessary to publish in Nature sub-journals, but if this carefully constructed platform lacks corresponding high-quality paper support, it always feels somewhat regrettable."*

Besides paper writing, Chen has also accumulated rich experience in applying for research grants. He firmly believes that the key to project applications lies in **precise, persuasive writing**. *"Actually, this is closely related to my consistently good Chinese language grades since childhood. Although my major was mathematics, my Chinese grades often ranked at the top of the school, so I feel more at ease when writing project application documents."* Chen further pointed out that being deeply influenced by geography during his PhD made his thinking more open and less prone to going astray, enabling him to propose more forward-looking and comprehensive questions from a scientific perspective when writing project proposals.

Additionally, Chen emphasized the influence of the culture of NNU's School of Geography: **"Pursuing excellence, continuous improvement."** He further explained: *"When teachers in the school write applications for the National Natural Science Foundation, they discuss during the topic selection stage, continue discussing during content writing, and after completion, they review each other's work to ensure the text reaches its best state. This collective sustained effort and close teamwork not only enhances external trust and recognition of our research projects but also enables us to stand out among a large number of fund applications."*

## 05 Deep Ties with CPGIS

During his postdoctoral period at CUHK, Chen Min established deep connections with CPGIS. Since arriving in Hong Kong in 2009, Professor Lin Hui strongly recommended that postdocs and students actively participate in various CPGIS activities and involve them in preparing for the CPGIS annual conference. Chen was particularly impressed by the **2012 CPGIS 20th Anniversary Annual Conference** held in Hong Kong. As a core member of the organizing team, he was not only responsible for picking up authoritative figures in academia such as Goodchild and Batty but also fully participated in multiple segments of the conference.

*"CPGIS provides a platform for unity, especially for overseas Chinese, which is very rare. When planning the annual conference back then, I was filled with enthusiasm just like everyone else."* Chen recalled with pride. *"Including the 25th anniversary commemorative conference held in Buffalo in 2017, I was also fortunate to participate in an important celebration of CPGIS."*

Besides organizing events, Chen also has deep affection for CPGIS's journal, *Annals of GIS*. In 2019, with Professor Lin Hui's retirement, the journal needed to find a new academic institution to serve as the journal office. At this time, Chen stepped forward and proactively undertook this task. After close communication with university leadership and the journal team, he successfully brought *Annals of GIS* to NNU. Throughout the handover process, Chen demonstrated his deep affection for CPGIS as well as a strong sense of responsibility and mission.

Regarding the future development of CPGIS, Chen deeply feels the difficulties currently faced by academic exchanges. Influenced by the international political environment, exchanges between Chinese and American teachers and students have become increasingly difficult, and are sometimes even constrained by policies prohibiting both sides from jointly organizing academic activities. Chen expressed his hope to overcome the difficulties of this period and promote continued in-depth academic exchanges between domestic and international scholars. *"At such a critical moment, if we choose to go our separate ways, I am afraid it will become very difficult to reunite in the future."* Chen said with concern. *"Although the current situation is complex, we should find other ways and channels to bring each other closer. When physical distance is no longer an obstacle, we can use other platforms to strengthen exchanges and cooperation, striving toward common goals."*

Speaking of plans for 2024–2025, Chen candidly stated, *"As President of CPGIS, my term is only one year. The goals and changes that can be achieved in such a short time are very limited, but I will still do my best to complete one or two important tasks during my term, hoping to contribute to the association's sustainable development."*

## 06 The Belief of Never Giving Up

When discussing how to balance research and daily life, as well as coping with stress, Chen shared his feelings with a very humble attitude: *"I must admit that I have not achieved a very good balance. When working in Hong Kong, I felt that the balance between life and work was relatively good. At that time, I did not have too much team burden, did not need to worry about applying for research grants, and could often play ball and hike. But since returning to the mainland, with the responsibility of leading a team and the pressure of considering the lab's future development, I have focused more on research."*

Chen candidly stated that despite various challenges, he does not feel heavy pressure: *"I just do my best, and as for the results, they are beyond my control."* This optimistic attitude, Chen said, was influenced by Professor Lin Hui. *"During those years in Hong Kong, I noticed that although Professor Lin Hui was busy, most of the time he was happy to have in-depth exchanges with students and push forward scientific research. His passion and interest in research were transmitted to us. I believe that as long as we truly love research, those pressures will naturally not be so hard to bear."*

&gt; **Chen Min's motto: "Never Give Up"**

Those who have received emails from Chen Min may have noticed that at the end of every email, there is always a simple yet powerful signature—**"Never Give Up."** This phrase originates from the passionate anime character Mitsui Hisashi in *Slam Dunk*. For Professor Chen, this is his life motto for many years. Whether being named one of the "Top Ten Young Science and Technology Stars of Jiangsu" in his youth, or now publishing important papers in high-impact academic journals, this motto of "Never Give Up" has always been the driving force that spurs Chen on and encourages his students to move forward.

*"I hope my team, my students, when facing any challenge or predicament, can uphold this spirit."* He also shared his life experience to inspire everyone: *"Actually, very often, challenges are not because there is truly no opportunity, but rather whether you give up easily. Some people, when submitting to high-level journals, lack confidence and feel the chance of success is slim, so they choose to give up. But as long as you bravely try and actively communicate with others, as long as reviewers see the value of your article, you still have a chance. The key is, when facing difficulties, are you willing to put in your maximum effort for it. For me, this spirit of 'Never Give Up' is not only an inspiring slogan but also the belief and practiced motivation on my research path."*
