# GIStory Issue 10 | Xin Yanan: Building Interpretable and Robust GeoAI with Causal Inference

## Guest Introduction

Xin Yanan is a postdoctoral researcher at ETH Zurich (Swiss Federal Institute of Technology). She received her bachelor's degree in Geographic Information Systems from Capital Normal University in 2013, her master's degree in Urban Spatial Analytics from the University of Pennsylvania in 2014, and her PhD in Geographic Information Science from Penn State University in 2020. She joined ETH Zurich as a postdoc in the same year and leads the **Mobility Information Engineering Lab**. Her main research interests include mobility trajectory data analysis, spatial causal analysis, interpretable machine learning, and sustainable transportation.

## 01 An Academic Journey That Began with a Book

*"It was a fortunate coincidence."* Dr. Xin Yanan said that while preparing her study abroad application, she happened to read Professor Ian McHarg's *Design with Nature* (1969). The book's perspective on combining landscape design with natural ecology, science, technology, art, and planning deeply attracted her. She was also drawn to the groundbreaking concept of the **"layer-cake method"** proposed in the book (which inspired the later development of GIS, particularly the birth of layer-by-layer analysis) and its application in planning. After researching and working hard, she ultimately decided to apply and successfully gained admission to the **Master of Urban Spatial Analytics** program at the University of Pennsylvania (UPenn) School of Design, where McHarg had once served. Although Ian McHarg had already left UPenn by then, his philosophy continued to influence the school's teaching and research.

At the same time, under the guidance of GIS pioneer **Professor Dana Tomlin** (founder of Map Algebra), GIS education at UPenn was committed to serving policy formulation and implementation through data analysis closely integrated with urban planning, transportation planning, public health, and historic preservation. At that time, Dr. Xin also received a direct PhD admission offer in GIS from another American university, but after careful deliberation, she chose UPenn's one-year master's program.

Due to the very compact academic schedule and its overlap with PhD application timelines, Dr. Xin's master's life was fulfilling and full of challenges. During her master's studies, she chose **transportation planning** as the main application area for her spatial analysis. The learning process sparked her desire to further understand how spatial data analysis could influence the operation of transportation projects and the formulation of transportation policies. Driven by curiosity and a thirst for knowledge, she chose to intern at the **Washington D.C. Department of Transportation** during her gap year between her master's and PhD. This valuable internship experience not only made her recognize the importance and value of her work and understand how what she did genuinely affected people's daily lives, but also deepened her love for research. Faced with various challenges in research, she became even more determined to continue pursuing a PhD. These credentials ultimately helped her successfully gain admission to the PhD program in the Department of Geography at **Penn State University**.

## 02 A Research Path Guided by Interest

Dr. Xin Yanan's paper at the **30th ACM SIGSPATIAL** [1] won the **"Best Vision Paper Award."** This article is recognized as having pioneered an entirely new research direction. Its core argument is that **combining causal inference can enhance the interpretability and robustness of ML/AI algorithms used for spatiotemporal data analysis.** The paper points out that although data **association** is very useful for prediction, association is not **causation**; conducting analysis and prediction using only associative relationships cannot provide us with strategies that can truly be used for policy intervention. Although machine learning or deep learning algorithms are increasingly widely applied in GIScience, black-box algorithms lack transparency and adaptability when applied across different spatiotemporal contexts. These limitations restrict the practical application of deep learning algorithms. To break through this bottleneck, Xin Yanan's team introduced the concept of **causal reasoning**, leveraging the explanatory power, transferability, and stability of causal relationships to compensate for the shortcomings of machine learning algorithms. They focused on exploring how to use causal inference and causal machine learning to handle **mobility analysis**, and proposed several research directions, such as using **causal intervention** to infer changes in prediction results when deep learning alters data representations, or using **counterfactual explanations** to analyze the influence of environmental factors (such as POIs and traffic conditions) in destination prediction for travel, among others. Beyond this, the paper also discussed using causal machine learning algorithms to improve model robustness when applied across different spatiotemporal environments.

During the paper's creation, Dr. Xin Yanan believed that their team's research direction and methodology could provide support for the development of GIScience, particularly GeoAI. Interest and confidence gave her a continuous stream of inspiration. Regarding how to write a high-quality paper, Dr. Xin also shared several insights:

- First, authors must be **full of interest and confidence** in the research content itself. If it is a topic you are deeply interested in, it is easy to generate a steady flow of inspiration during the writing process.
- Second, the article should have a **clear main thread and logic**, helping to determine the core discussion and subdivided key points.
- Third, **solid research and knowledge reserves** are needed to ensure that the paper's discussion achieves the necessary breadth and depth.

*"For example, in this vision paper, we not only summarized the current challenges of deep learning in studying spatiotemporal data and some progress in causal machine learning research, but we also pointed out specific promising research directions and applicable methods, hoping to provide some inspiration for readers interested in this field."*

## 03 Reflections on Research Life

Regarding how to find good research topics in academia, Xin Yanan believes that **asking more questions** is key. For example, when reading articles, consider where the strengths and weaknesses of deep learning lie, why it performs so well, and what the practical significance of the research is. Regarding the importance of mathematics in research, Xin Yanan firmly believes that mathematics not only provides powerful assistance for her research but also, on a broader level, helps her cultivate rigorous logical thinking. She considers mathematics as a research language and art of thinking; if one can master it proficiently, it can help open up broader research fields.

Xin Yanan says that life at ETH is distinctive. It not only has a pleasant working and living environment but also blends with Switzerland's beautiful nature, making everyone passionate about hiking and skiing. As a PhD student or postdoc, you will find that ETH places great emphasis on **work-life balance**. Everyone has **five weeks of annual leave** each year, and the school encourages people to take time off, avoiding excessive work on weekends. Having experienced the transition from PhD to Postdoc, Xin Yanan deeply feels that there is a great difference between the two. During the PhD stage, people focus more on their own research, while as a Postdoc, besides continuing to do one's own research well, one also needs to learn how to manage the lab, apply for funding, organize teams, lead research projects, mentor students, and teach. Of course, this depends on the requirements that the chosen research group has for postdocs. Therefore, choosing a lab also needs to be decided based on personal interests and long-term plans. *"For those friends interested in pursuing an academic career, I suggest starting to develop your abilities in applying for and managing research projects as early as possible."*

## 04 Message

Dr. Xin Yanan has the following messages to encourage students at different stages:

- **For undergraduates:** I hope that during your university years, you actively explore and experience, enrich your university life, and find what you truly love.
- **For master's students:** Maintain your curiosity and proactivity, and find your own development direction. Beyond your studies, actively seek opportunities for corporate internships or research assistant positions to enrich your professional knowledge and experience, so as to better adapt to the next stage of study or work.
- **For PhD students:** On the path of research, you will face some confusion and setbacks, but they are also accompanied by the joy and hope brought by the acquisition of knowledge. In this process, maintain a peaceful mindset, learn to seek resources, ask for help, and actively face the problems you encounter.
