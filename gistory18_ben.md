Ben Keel is currently a Data Analyst at Brandywine Realty Trust, holding a Master's degree in Urban Spatial Analytics from the University of Pennsylvania. His academic research focuses on graphic and communication design, using data science as a core method for problem-solving. Across multiple projects, Ben has dedicated himself to addressing various communication challenges, such as online presentation of complex news, three-dimensional spatial display, and signage and navigation system design. He has assisted journalist Norman Oder in reporting on Brooklyn real estate dynamics and participated in designing exhibitions at the U.S. Capitol Visitor Center in Washington, D.C. Additionally, Ben has continuously tracked and reported on the progress of the Pacific Park mega-development project in Brooklyn through infographics. Currently, he is committed to combining user-centered design philosophy with exploration of social issues and the commercial real estate sector, with a particular focus on practical applications of geospatial data analysis, visualization expression, and front-end development technologies.


## 01 Discovering Design: From Visual Communication to Spatial Narrative

Ben's early career was like an urban odyssey—Boston, Cincinnati, Washington, D.C.—each internship leaving its unique mark.

At an architectural design firm in Boston, he and his team focused on large-scale visual projects for the built environment, such as signage systems, place-making, and wall murals. He recalled: *"We didn't just design on computers; we also personally assembled the entire 'books' mural for the Boston Public Library."*

Beyond that, his work content was similar to most internship experiences—refining and perfecting design systems already established by the team. For instance, every sign in the office, every arrow in the parking lot, needed someone to implement the design, and these concrete, hands-on tasks were often delegated to interns.

&gt; *"It is precisely this process that allows young practitioners to solidify their professional foundations—repetitive practice. Whether in design, programming, data integration, or other professions, it is all the same. I was fortunate that the projects I participated in at that time could eventually become physical achievements locally. Seeing my own designs bring impact to the real world made me proud."* Ben shared his internship experience.

Through participating in community projects and engaging with physical media such as books and detailed typography, he gradually realized that design is not just websites, color schemes, or posters, but can penetrate urban fabric and become a medium for telling community stories. He elaborated:

&gt; *"Design is not just a narrative method, but also a tool for connecting people, spaces, and communities."*


## 02 A Pivotal Turning Point: The Shift from Design to Housing Issues

An internship experience in Boston unexpectedly caused a subtle shift in his career trajectory.

At that time, he participated in an enhancement project for a new school. The design team invested tremendous effort, hoping the project could not only serve charter school students but also become a shared public resource for the community. However, due to some residents' concerns that the project would exacerbate traffic congestion and affect local children's schooling environment after completion, strong community opposition emerged, ultimately preventing the project from advancing at the original site.

&gt; *"Why would a well-intentioned project encounter such resistance?"*

This question planted a seed in his heart. So he began searching for similar cases where residents' and developers' goals did not align. Under the advice of his advisor Lisa Barlow during his internship at the University of Cincinnati, he turned his attention to the **"Atlantic Yards"** development project in Brooklyn. The project promised mixed-income housing, basketball courts, and office space, yet unexpectedly fell into delays and controversy. Through in-depth research, Ben realized that the problem often lay not in the design itself:

&gt; *"Many real estate projects struggle to simultaneously satisfy the different goals of community groups, local governments, and developers. And the lack of trust and poor communication between them further exacerbates the difficulty of advancing projects."*

It was from this point that Ben gradually shifted his focus from visual communication to infographics and data visualization, eventually entering the intersection of data and real estate, hoping to participate in urban development processes in a more direct way.

During his time in Washington, D.C., he also participated in a volunteer affordable housing project called **"Just Homes"** initiated by a local church. Through this experience, he not only gained a deep understanding of D.C.'s housing situation but also developed a more solid grasp of housing policy and the real estate market, further solidifying his determination to make this field his long-term career direction.

A few years later, the pandemic slowed the world's pace, also giving Ben more time to examine his future. By then he had been working in graphic design for five years, experiencing a period of pandemic-induced reflection and contemplation. Like many others, he craved change. After much consideration, due to Penn's rich resources in housing policy courses, he applied for the data visualization track under the University of Pennsylvania's Master of Urban Spatial Analytics program, thus embarking on a new path that could integrate design, data, and social issues.


## 03 The Learning Path from Designer to Data Analyst: Continuing the Passion for Housing Issues

During graduate school, Ben absorbed knowledge in the affordable housing field like a sponge. Whether in classroom projects, freelance work, or late-night independent research, he was constantly exploring one question: how can data and design jointly promote more equitable urban development?

Today, he serves as a Data Analyst at **Brandywine Realty Trust**, Philadelphia's largest Class A office building developer, affilated with the Operations and Development department. His graphic design background makes him adept at data visualization, while his current position further requires him to complete multi-source data integration and analysis process design to support the company's development, asset management, and leasing operations.

This position is a newly established function within the company, integrating data engineering, analysis, and visualization, and closely interfacing with business departments. Since the team needs to achieve automated analysis of multiple heterogeneous data sources, Ben's geospatial analysis skills are perfectly suited to help build highly automated systems. For example, he tracks construction permit data in real-time for cities such as **Philadelphia and Austin** through Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), promptly grasping development dynamics for office buildings and public spaces.

At the same time, Ben continues to produce data visualizations and interactive charts for journalist **Norman Oder's** newsletter **"Learning from Atlantic Yards."** Norman's blog is dedicated to distilling broader issues from this large Brooklyn development project and summarizing daily reporting content; Ben assists in transforming complex information involving building affordability, ownership structures, and historical evolution into clear visual expressions. Based on this, they construct three-dimensional models showing the visual effects of planned buildings at the street level, used to compare the impacts of different development schemes.

This accountability-focused reporting is particularly critical at present: the project is facing a critical deadline for affordable housing delivery on **May 31, 2025**. If the developer fails to complete the additional **876 units of affordable housing** on time, they may face fines of millions of dollars per month. However, another possibility exists: the New York State government, out of consideration for the business environment, may choose not to enforce the penalty clause and instead seek other partners to advance the project.

Ben hopes that through the blog's continuous reporting and clear information communication, the community can be helped to timely understand project progress, especially in the context of the developer's unfulfilled promises, providing a basis for whether the government should intervene, thereby pushing the project to eventual completion.


## 04 The Career of a Data Analyst: Making Data Truly Serve Decision-Making

In Ben's view, the value of data analysis lies not in building complex models, but in being able to extract meaningful insights from massive datasets and convey them to decision-makers in a concise and understandable way. This often means condensing complex analysis results into a single page of clear, focused slides that precisely address key questions. As he puts it: *"Executives don't need to know the technical details of data extraction; they only need to know the core conclusions—such as, 'Should we build a building here?'"*

Over the past two years, Ben has helped the company establish a standardized data engineering workflow for building open data databases. He adopted an **Extract, Load, Transform (ELT)** architecture: extracting data through Python, loading via Shell scripts, processing spatial data with GDAL, and completing data transformation with dbt. This workflow enables the team to fully utilize open-source tools such as PostgreSQL and QGIS, reducing dependence on expensive proprietary platforms. By advancing workflow automation, Ben has secured more time for the team to focus on strategic thinking and decision-making.


## 05 Job Hunting and Communication Advice: Know Your Audience, Express Clearly

When Ben reflects on his own transition journey, he wants to share a few insights with all peers developing in the workplace:

**Know Your Audience:** Whether facing executives or government officials, first clarify the other party's background and needs, and adjust your language and visual presentation accordingly.

**Build Networks:** Participate more in industry exchange activities, actively building connections with peers. Networking should not be solely for the purpose of "finding a job," but should genuinely interest you in others' experiences, learning and growing from them.

**Showcase Your Work:** In the job search process, a well-structured, visually-oriented portfolio is very important. Ben suggests organizing displays by skill categories, making it easy for recruiters to quickly understand your capabilities and match them with position requirements.

**Express Clearly:** In communication, try to avoid jargon. For example, rather than saying "I performed a buffer analysis," say "I analyzed the range people can reach within a five-minute walk." The focus is on conveying the practical significance of your work to the audience, not the technical methods used.

**Professionalism:** In the workplace, clear communication, reliable execution, and proactive sense of responsibility often win more trust than purely technical abilities. Even if tasks are delayed, proactively explain reasons and propose feasible follow-up plans—this is precisely the professionalism embodied by an urban data analyst.
