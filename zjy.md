# GIStory Issue 7 | Zhang Jingyuan: From GISer to Big Tech—A Job Seeker's Journey

## Guest Introduction

Zhang Jingyuan is the lead maintainer of the GISphere website and currently works as a front-end development engineer at Tencent. He received his bachelor's degree from the Department of Geographic Information Science at Nanjing University in 2020, and his master's degree in GeoInformation Science from The Chinese University of Hong Kong in 2021. During his studies, he interned at companies including Esri China and SF Express.

## 01 Career Exploration: From GIS to Front-End

As one of the few seniors in the GISphere team working at a major domestic internet company, we had long planned to produce an episode of GIStory to chat with Jingyuan about his experience job hunting in the internet industry. By a fortunate coincidence, we completed this interview last month, delving deeply into how to find work opportunities in the internet sector.

Unlike many students who resolutely switched to computer science during their undergraduate years, Jingyuan, who studied in the Department of Geographic Information Science at Nanjing University, in fact never once considered transferring to the Computer Science department. In his own words, "GIS itself is a very interesting and useful discipline."

Similar to many people's experiences, during his freshman year, Jingyuan had no prior programming experience. The university's mandatory C language course was his introduction to programming. What truly sparked his interest was a technical course he took in his sophomore year—**Geographic Databases**, taught by Professor She Jiangfeng at Nanjing University. This course laid the foundation for his path toward real programming and development. In this course, Professor She not only introduced the operational statements for database CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete), but also taught how to connect databases, back-end logic, and front-end interaction together. For the final capstone project, students were required to independently complete a full application. In this application, each student could choose different content using various technologies according to their own interests. Through his own research, Jingyuan added web animation content on top of basic application operations, resulting in a noticeably improved user interaction experience compared to his classmates. This capstone project not only earned him a high grade but also opened the door to front-end development for him.

Although he gradually developed an interest in programming and development, Jingyuan never considered transferring to a computer science major. In his view, most of the professional knowledge required for general programming development can be obtained through self-study online. What he was more interested in was **how to better present content to all users**. In this regard, the GIS major actually has unique advantages. An excellent GIS student not only needs to be familiar with the application of various GIS software but also typically has good aesthetics in order to visualize large amounts of spatial data. At the same time, the GIS major is not as "cutthroat" as computer science, allowing him more time and resources to work on what he wants to do. Therefore, his GIS academic background was more of a boost for Jingyuan, so when facing major specialization choices, he unhesitatingly chose the GIS major.

However, after becoming a GIS student, Jingyuan found that the Department of Geographic Information Science at Nanjing University focused more on research combining GIS with physical and human geography. During this period, following his advisor's guidance, he broadly tried different directions including **remote sensing algorithms based on MATLAB** and **Unity 3D development combined with human geography**, which to some extent honed his comprehensive abilities. At the same time, he continued to explore the front-end field, trying to complete coding assignments for various professional courses using front-end technologies as much as possible, and developed the habit of writing blogs and developing personal websites to exercise his programming skills.

## 02 Failure and Success

**Opportunity favors the prepared mind.** After clarifying his interest direction in his junior year, Jingyuan soon encountered an opportunity: an internship at **ByteDance**. ByteDance has good cooperation with Nanjing University and recruits a large number of interns there every year. After seeing the job description, Jingyuan directly submitted his resume and soon faced his first front-end development position interview.

However, life is never that smooth. At that time, Jingyuan's development skills were mainly self-taught; he had not fully covered all syntaxes and was not familiar with the development technologies used by enterprises. Submitting his resume was more out of a trial mentality, so unsurprisingly, he encountered failure. At that time, the ByteDance interviewer first asked him an algorithm question, but Jingyuan was not yet proficient in the required JS syntax, and after writing for a long time, the syntax still wouldn't run. The first interview ended quickly in failure, but it was not without gain. After the interview ended, the ByteDance interviewer generously provided Jingyuan with guidance. From the interviewer, he truly understood what knowledge and skills major internet companies actually need.

After this "failed" yet not entirely failed interview, Jingyuan finally saw a clear path ahead. Thereafter, he began to **cultivate his front-end development abilities in a targeted manner**, including systematic learning of complete JavaScript syntax, extensive problem-solving on LeetCode, and learning commonly used enterprise development frameworks such as React and Vue. After completing his technical accumulation, internship opportunities came one after another. During his final undergraduate year and first year of master's studies, he interned successively at **Esri China**, a leading company in the GIS industry, and **SF Express**, engaging in related front-end development work. During these two years of internships, he gradually realized that in China, the overall demand for front-end development in the GIS industry is relatively small, and front-end development is not the core business focus. Therefore, he ultimately decided to still focus his job search on the internet industry. In the summer of 2021, he came to **Tencent** for an internship through an interview. Finally, after completing his master's studies at the end of 2021, Jingyuan joined Tencent through internship conversion, officially becoming a front-end development engineer and achieving his long-standing career goal.

## 03 Work Experience at Internet Companies

As an engineer with work experience at both a leading GIS enterprise and a leading internet company, Jingyuan has experienced different work paradigms over the past few years. In this interview, he also shared with us his insights from working in different tracks and fields.

First, Jingyuan mentioned some respective advantages and disadvantages of domestic GIS companies and the internet industry. For GIS companies, as a niche field, the job competition is not as fierce as in internet companies. However, traditional GIS companies have some inherent industry problems, such as being prone to bottlenecks in business models and having relatively low overall demand for technical positions. Therefore, he believes that considering the current dominance of **to-G (government-facing) business** in traditional GIS companies, technical ability is not the most critical requirement if you want to join a GIS company for related work. Instead, some other professional qualities, such as **design ability, sales ability**, etc., may be very important, especially abilities needed when directly facing clients. He particularly pointed out that many GIS students have good system design thinking and communication skills, so they can consider developing in these directions.

Next, he shared his work content and experience. Recently, Jingyuan's work has focused on **low-code technology**, i.e., using a small amount of code and configuration files to complete the development of a large number of simple pages to meet the needs of different enterprise-level users. He particularly noted that although the short-term income of engineers at major companies is relatively substantial, they must also bear the **pressure and risks** that come with the job. Working in the industry, the pressure of new technology iteration is continuous and high-intensity. For example, although languages like JavaScript are already quite stable, various "wheels" (reinvented tools/frameworks) constantly emerge both inside and outside companies. In these technological iterations, practitioners must continuously absorb new knowledge and complete development in new ways, which also demands **lifelong learning capabilities**.

For students currently studying GIS and hoping to find a place in industry, Jingyuan provided **three pieces of advice**:

1. **Do not blindly follow the trend of switching to the internet industry.** Each era has its own style. After years of development, the internet industry has now entered a relatively stable phase with little business expansion and few open positions. Switching to coding blindly just for high salaries or following trends is not necessarily a good choice.

2. **If you want to work in industry, you don't necessarily have to take a technical position.** In fact, for many companies, besides technology, there are many other positions, such as **product, marketing**, etc., which are all very good choices with corresponding competency requirements and growth paths. You can consider developing in these directions and cultivating your corresponding competitiveness.

3. **For students who want to enter the workforce, you must intern early.** Internships can not only accumulate experience and enrich your resume but also allow you to familiarize yourself with a industry's work environment in advance to judge whether you are truly suitable for the position.

## 04 Message

The development of industry changes rapidly. Drawing from his own experience and the changing situation under the current landscape, Jingyuan encourages students who have not yet entered the workforce to consider not only job compensation but also **personal strengths and preferences**. Explore more in practice, then choose a suitable position among the many available in the job market, so that you can overcome obstacles and find joy in your chosen path. For students who have already joined the workforce, they need to **continuously improve their comprehensive abilities and work skills**, keep up with the times, and enhance their **risk resilience** when facing difficulties.
